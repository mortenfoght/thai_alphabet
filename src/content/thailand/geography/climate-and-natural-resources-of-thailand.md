# Climate and Natural Resources of Thailand: Monsoons, Forests, and Minerals

*A guide to Thailand's tropical monsoon climate and its natural resources, from rubber and rice to natural gas, tin, and shrinking forest cover.*

The climate and natural resources of Thailand are closely linked, since the country's tropical monsoon weather patterns directly shape what grows, what gets mined, and how the economy sustains itself across four very different regions. Thailand's location just north of the equator, combined with seasonal reversals in wind direction, produces a climate of alternating wet and dry extremes that has supported one of Southeast Asia's most productive agricultural bases while also straining forests, water tables, and soil over the past half-century ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)).

## Thailand's Tropical Monsoon Climate

Thailand's climate is classified as tropical, governed by two seasonal monsoon systems that produce three broadly recognized seasons: rainy, cool, and hot ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/); [Wikipedia — Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok)). The southwest monsoon brings warm, humid, cloudy conditions from mid-May to September, delivering the bulk of the year's rainfall, while the northeast monsoon brings cooler, drier air from November to mid-March ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)). A shorter hot season bridges the two monsoons from roughly mid-March to mid-May. The southern isthmus is the exception to this pattern, remaining hot and humid year-round regardless of season ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)).

### Bangkok as a Climate Benchmark

Bangkok illustrates the national pattern clearly: average temperatures range from a December low near 23°C to an April high near 35.7°C, with an annual mean around 28.9°C ([Wikipedia — Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok)). September is typically the wettest month, averaging around 336 millimeters of rainfall, while December is the driest, often receiving barely 10 millimeters, and the city's total annual rainfall generally falls between 1,600 and 1,700 millimeters depending on the data source ([Wikipedia — Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok)). The rainy season runs from mid-May through October before the dry, cool northeast monsoon takes over into February ([Wikipedia — Bangkok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangkok)).

### Regional Climate Variation

Climate conditions vary meaningfully by region. Northern Thailand, including Chiang Mai, experiences the most pronounced seasonal swings, with genuinely cool, sometimes near-freezing nights at higher elevations in December and January, alongside a "burning season" from January through April that degrades air quality through agricultural fires ([Thailand Privilege](https://www.thailandprivilege.co.th/why-thailand/climate-in-thailand-guide)). The Andaman coast, including Phuket, receives its heaviest rain from May through October, while the Gulf coast, including Koh Samui, has a reversed pattern, peaking from October through December ([Thailand Privilege](https://www.thailandprivilege.co.th/why-thailand/climate-in-thailand-guide)).

## Natural Hazards and Environmental Pressures

Thailand's most persistent natural hazards include periodic droughts and land subsidence around Bangkok, driven by depletion of the underlying water table as the city has expanded ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)). Ongoing environmental issues include air pollution from vehicle emissions, water pollution from industrial and organic waste, water scarcity, soil erosion, deforestation, and threats to wildlife from illegal hunting ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)).

## Land Use: Agriculture and Forest Cover

Roughly 41% of Thailand's land is devoted to agriculture, including about 31% arable land and 9% permanent crops, while forest historically covered around 37% of the country as of the last comprehensive Factbook estimate ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)). More recent national data put forest cover closer to 31–32% of total land area, reflecting continued net forest loss even as the government has set a long-standing policy target of restoring cover to 40% ([AFoCO](https://afocosec.org/knowledge/country-information-hub/thailand/); [Deforestation in Thailand — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation_in_Thailand)). The World Bank's FAO-sourced figures suggest a slightly higher, roughly 38–39% share as recently as 2023, illustrating how forest statistics vary depending on measurement methodology and the year of assessment ([TheGlobalEconomy.com](https://www.theglobaleconomy.com/Thailand/forest_area/)). Northern Thailand holds the largest share of remaining forest, accounting for more than half the national total, while permanent agricultural expansion remains the leading driver of continued tree cover loss ([AFoCO](https://afocosec.org/knowledge/country-information-hub/thailand/); [Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2025/10/protected-areas-hit-hard-as-mekong-countries-forest-cover-shrank-in-2024/)). In 2024 alone, Thailand lost more than 140,000 hectares of tree cover, though the vast majority of that loss occurred outside formally protected areas, which cover about 10 million hectares nationwide ([Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2025/10/protected-areas-hit-hard-as-mekong-countries-forest-cover-shrank-in-2024/)).

## Mineral and Energy Resources

Thailand's officially recognized natural resources include tin, rubber, natural gas, tungsten, tantalum, timber, lead, fish, gypsum, lignite, fluorite, and arable land ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)).

### Natural Gas and the Gulf of Thailand

Natural gas extracted from offshore fields in the Gulf of Thailand has long underpinned the country's energy sector, with proved reserves across Gulf fields historically estimated near 5.94 trillion cubic feet alongside significant condensate reserves ([USAID](https://pdf.usaid.gov/pdf_docs/PNAAQ427.pdf)). Domestic gas production has been in gradual decline in recent years, pushing Thailand toward greater reliance on imported liquefied natural gas to meet electricity demand ([ASEAN Centre for Energy](https://aseanenergy.org/news-clipping/thailand-sets-its-sights-on-larger-lng-share/)).

### Tin Mining and Gemstones

Tin mining shaped the economic history of the South, particularly Phuket, where tin deposits drew Chinese and European commercial interest starting in the 19th century and financed much of the island's early development before tourism eclipsed mining as the dominant industry ([Phuket Live](https://live.phuketindex.com/the-history-of-phukets-tin-mining-industry-2093.html)).

## Agricultural Resources: Rubber, Rice, and Fisheries

Thailand is consistently ranked among the world's leading producers and exporters of natural rubber, a crop concentrated heavily in the southern peninsula and, increasingly, in the northeast ([Thailand Board of Investment](https://www.boi.go.th/upload/content/TIR_AUGUST_PROOF_10_44271.pdf)). Rice cultivation remains a cornerstone of the rural economy nationwide, especially in the Central Plain's fertile floodplain and in Isan's paddy fields, where sticky rice covers roughly 60% of cultivated land ([Wikipedia — Isan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan)). Fisheries, both marine and freshwater, round out the resource base, drawing on the country's extensive coastlines along the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea as well as inland rivers such as the Chao Phraya and Mekong ([CIA World Factbook archive](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/about/archives/2021/countries/thailand/)).

## Balancing Growth and Conservation

Thailand's natural endowments have powered decades of economic transformation, but the same monsoon rainfall, fertile soils, and mineral wealth that built the modern economy now face mounting pressure from urban expansion, water table depletion, and continued agricultural conversion of forest land. Government targets to lift forest cover back to 40% of national territory, alongside expanded protected-area enforcement, signal an ongoing effort to balance resource extraction with conservation as Thailand's climate and land-use pressures continue to evolve ([AFoCO](https://afocosec.org/knowledge/country-information-hub/thailand/)).
