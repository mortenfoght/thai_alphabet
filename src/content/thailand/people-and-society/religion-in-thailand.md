# Religion in Thailand: Buddhism, Islam, and Religious Minorities

*Religion in Thailand is dominated by Theravada Buddhism at roughly 93–95% of the population, with Islam the largest minority faith, concentrated in the southern border provinces.*

Religion in Thailand shapes daily life more visibly than in almost any other country in Southeast Asia, from the saffron-robed monks collecting morning alms to the spirit houses standing outside offices and homes. Theravada Buddhism is the overwhelming majority faith, practiced by somewhere between 90% and 95% of the population depending on the survey, giving Thailand the largest national Buddhist population on Earth ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/06/09/buddhist-population-change/)). Yet Thailand's constitution establishes no official state religion, and the country legally recognizes five faiths — Buddhism, Islam, Christianity, Hinduism (Brahmanism), and Sikhism — reflecting a religious landscape that, while heavily Buddhist, is more layered than the headline statistics suggest ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)).

## Buddhism: The Dominant Faith

Theravada Buddhism has shaped Thai law, culture, and monarchy since at least the Sukhothai period in the 13th century, and today more than 90% of Thais identify as Buddhist ([Wikipedia — Buddhism in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism_in_Thailand)). Thailand's 2018 national census recorded Buddhists at roughly 93.5% of the population, essentially unchanged from the 93.6% figure recorded in the 2010 census, though some independent estimates suggest the true figure may run slightly lower once non-census surveys are factored in ([Wikipedia — Religion in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Thailand); [SAWASDEE THAILAND](https://thailand.go.th/page/religion)). By raw numbers, Thailand has roughly 67–68 million Buddhists, more than any other country in the world, and Pew Research Center data shows Thailand's Buddhist population share (94%) trails only Cambodia (97%) among Buddhist-majority nations ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/06/09/buddhist-population-change/)).

### Buddhism and the Monarchy

Buddhism has deep constitutional ties to the Thai crown: the reigning King is constitutionally required to be Buddhist and is formally recognized as the "upholder of religions" in Thailand, even though the constitution does not name Buddhism the state religion ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)). King Mongkut, or Rama IV, is widely credited with reforming and formalizing Buddhist practice in the 19th century, including founding the reformist Dhammayuttika monastic order that remains influential in Thai Buddhism today. The constitution empowers the state to specifically patronize and protect Theravada Buddhism through education and measures intended to prevent its "desecration," a level of institutional support not extended equally to Thailand's other recognized faiths ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)).

### Regional Variation

Buddhist adherence is not uniform across Thailand. The northeastern Isan region records the highest Buddhist share of any region, close to 99.8% of the population according to 2010 census breakdowns, while Bangkok, with its larger immigrant, Muslim, and Christian communities, records a noticeably lower Buddhist share of roughly 92–94% ([Wikipedia — Southern Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Thailand); [Wikipédia — Religion en Thaïlande](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_en_Tha%C3%AFlande)). Southern Thailand records the lowest regional Buddhist share, at roughly 75%, reflecting the concentration of the Muslim population in that region ([Wikipedia — Southern Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Thailand)).

## Islam: Thailand's Largest Religious Minority

Islam is Thailand's largest minority religion, though estimates of the exact Muslim population vary considerably by source and methodology, ranging from roughly 4.3% to as high as 10–12% of the national population ([Wikipedia — Islam in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Thailand)). Thailand's official 2018 government survey put the figure at 5.4%, while a Thai government source cited in 2024 estimated approximately 7.5 million Thai Muslims, or roughly 12% of the population — a wide range that reflects both differing survey methods and long-standing debate over how mixed-heritage and non-practicing Muslims are counted ([SAWASDEE THAILAND](https://thailand.go.th/page/religion); [Wikipedia — Islam in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam_in_Thailand)).

### Concentration in the Deep South

Roughly 99% of Thai Muslims are Sunni, with a small Shia community concentrated in Bangkok and Nakhon Si Thammarat province ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)). While Muslims are found throughout the country, they form overwhelming majorities in the three southernmost border provinces — Pattani, Yala, and Narathiwat — where the Muslim population, predominantly ethnic Malay, ranges between roughly 80% and 90% of local residents ([Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/islam-education-and-reform-in-southern-thailand/islam-and-malaymuslim-identity-in-thailand/84930DE829BC9990FCB633EC9FB0513D); [Melbourne Asia Review](https://www.melbourneasiareview.edu.au/the-muslim-ethno-religiouscape-of-thailand-can-be-accommodated-peacefully/)). Despite this concentration, most Thai Muslims — more than 80% by some estimates — actually live outside these four southern border provinces, spread across the rest of the country including Bangkok ([Melbourne Asia Review](https://www.melbourneasiareview.edu.au/the-muslim-ethno-religiouscape-of-thailand-can-be-accommodated-peacefully/)). The deep south's Malay Muslim identity is tied to the history of the former Sultanate of Pattani, and the region has experienced a long-running separatist insurgency since the early 2000s, driven partly by tensions between Malay Muslim identity and the Thai Buddhist state.

## Christianity and Other Minority Faiths

Christianity is a small but longstanding minority religion in Thailand, accounting for roughly 1–1.2% of the population, with more than half of Thai Christians identifying as Roman Catholic and the remainder largely Protestant ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)). Christian missionary activity, particularly from the 19th century onward, found particular success among some hill tribe communities in the north, including segments of the Karen population.

Hinduism (often practiced alongside Brahmanism in Thai court ritual) and Sikhism together make up a small fraction of the population, well under 1% combined, and are followed mainly by communities of Indian descent concentrated in Bangkok and other urban centers ([WorldAtlas](https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/religious-beliefs-in-thailand.html)). Despite their small numbers, Hindu and Brahmanic influences run deep in Thai royal ceremony and popular religious practice — many Thai Buddhists also revere Hindu deities such as Brahma and Ganesha alongside Buddhist teachings, a syncretism dating back to the Khmer and Indian cultural influence on early Southeast Asian kingdoms ([Wikipedia — Religion in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_Thailand)). Smaller communities of Confucians and adherents of Chinese folk religion also exist within Thailand's large Thai Chinese population, alongside Sikh communities descended from Punjabi immigrants.

## Legal Framework and Religious Freedom

Thailand's constitution formally prohibits discrimination based on religious belief and protects the right to profess, observe, or practice any religion, provided that exercise does not threaten state security ([U.S. Department of State](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-report-on-international-religious-freedom/thailand/)). The Ministry of Culture's Religious Affairs Department oversees official recognition and registration of religious organizations, and government figures suggest that atheists or those without religious affiliation make up less than 1% of the population — a strikingly low figure that reflects both genuine religious adherence and the extent to which religious identity is treated as a near-default category in Thai civil life ([Wikipedia — Freedom of religion in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_religion_in_Thailand)).
