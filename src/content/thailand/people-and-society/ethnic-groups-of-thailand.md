# Ethnic Groups of Thailand: A Guide to the Kingdom's Peoples

*Thailand's ethnic groups range from the Central Thai majority to Lao-speaking Isan communities, Thai Chinese, Malay Muslims, and northern hill tribes.*

Thailand is often described as ethnically homogeneous, but that impression obscures a far more layered reality. The ethnic groups of Thailand include not just the Central Thai who dominate government and media, but tens of millions of Lao-speaking Isan people, a large Thai Chinese population woven into business and urban life, Malay Muslims concentrated in the deep south, and dozens of smaller Tai, Austroasiatic, Sino-Tibetan, and Austronesian communities spread across the northern highlands and southern peninsula. Officially, the Thai government recognizes 62 distinct ethnolinguistic communities within its borders ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)), a figure that reflects a diversity often flattened by decades of nation-building policy that classified nearly everyone as simply "Thai."

## Why Official Statistics Undercount Diversity

Thai census data has long presented an artificially uniform picture of the population. The 1904 Siamese census deliberately omitted Lao ethnic identity to discourage French colonial claims on Lao-speaking territory, pushing the official Thai population share to 85%, then 89% by 1912 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). From the 1960s onward, census figures described up to 99% of the population as ethnically Thai, even though independent research from the same era found only about a third of the population spoke Central Thai natively, with more than half speaking Lao dialects such as Isan ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). This traces to early-20th-century nation-building, when nationalist figures like Luang Wichitwathakan promoted a pan-Tai racial identity folding distinct groups into one national category, reinforced by the 1939 renaming of the country from Siam to Thailand ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). Thai censuses still do not disaggregate most ethnic categories, so the figures below draw on academic and NGO research rather than official statistics alone.

## The Tai-Speaking Majority

Roughly 85% of Thailand's population belongs to one of more than 30 distinct Tai ethnic groups ([Wikipedia — Peopling of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peopling_of_Thailand)), but this Tai-speaking majority itself splits into several large, regionally distinct populations.

### Central Thai

The Central Thai, native to the Chao Phraya River basin and Bangkok, form the group most associated with the national language, government, and dominant culture. Estimates using 1997 Mahidol University linguistic mapping data put the Central Thai population (together with the related Khorat Thai) at approximately 20.65 million, or roughly 34% of the population counted at the time ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)).

### Lao and Isan Peoples

The largest single ethnic bloc in Thailand is arguably the Lao-speaking population of the northeastern Isan region, estimated at around 15 million people, or roughly a quarter of the national population, once all Lao-related subgroups are combined ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). Isan people speak a language closely related to — and largely mutually intelligible with — the Lao of neighboring Laos, a legacy of the region's history as part of the Lao-speaking world before the Mekong River became a political border after the Franco-Siamese crisis of 1893 ([Wikipedia — Isan language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isan_language)).

### Northern and Southern Thai

The Khon Muang, or Northern Thai, descend from the old Lanna kingdom centered on Chiang Mai and number around 6 million people, about 10% of the population ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). The Pak Tai, or Southern Thai, inhabit the Malay Peninsula portion of the country and number around 4.5 million, roughly 7.5% of the population, with a culture and dialect shaped by centuries of contact with Malay communities ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)).

## Thai Chinese: The Largest Diaspora Community

Thailand is home to the largest overseas Chinese population in the world. Estimates place the Thai Chinese community, including those of full and partial Chinese descent, at roughly 9 to 9.5 million people, or 11–14% of the national population as of the early 2010s ([Wikipedia — Thai Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_Chinese)). The majority trace their ancestry to Teochew-speaking migrants from China's Guangdong province, who began settling in large numbers during the 19th century and came to dominate trade in Bangkok's Chinatown district; Teochew Chinese still account for more than half of the Thai Chinese population today ([Wikipedia — Teochew people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teochew_people)). Unlike Chinese communities in some neighboring countries, most Thai Chinese have intermarried and assimilated deeply into Thai society over generations, adopting Thai names, language, and Buddhist practice while retaining elements of Chinese festivals, ancestor veneration, and family business traditions.

## Malay Muslims of the Deep South

The Malay ethnic group, concentrated overwhelmingly in the three southernmost border provinces of Pattani, Yala, and Narathiwat, forms the majority population in that region despite being a small minority nationally — estimated at roughly 900,000 to as many as 2–4 million people depending on the source and classification used ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). This community is ethnically, linguistically, and religiously distinct from the Thai Buddhist majority, speaking Pattani Malay and practicing Islam, and its historical roots trace to the pre-colonial Malay Sultanate of Pattani, which was absorbed into the Siamese state in stages during the 19th and early 20th centuries.

## Khmer, Kuy, and Other Northeastern Minorities

Along Thailand's border with Cambodia, particularly in the Isan provinces of Surin, Buriram, and Sisaket, live substantial communities of ethnic Khmer (Northern Khmer), estimated at around 1.2 to 1.4 million people, alongside roughly 400,000 speakers of the related Kuy (or Suay) language ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). These Austroasiatic-language communities predate the arrival of Tai-speaking peoples in the region and represent some of the oldest continuous settlements in what is now Thailand.

## Hill Tribes of the North

Thailand's mountainous northern provinces, especially Chiang Mai, Chiang Rai, and Mae Hong Son, are home to a mosaic of highland peoples collectively known as "hill tribes" or, in Thai, *chao khao*. The Thai government's Hill Tribe Welfare Committee has historically recognized nine main groups: the Karen, Hmong, Lahu, Akha, Lisu, Mien (Yao), Lua, Lawa, and Khamu ([Wikipedia — Hill tribe (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_tribe_(Thailand))).

The **Karen** are by far the largest hill tribe, numbering approximately 320,000 to 510,000 people and accounting for roughly half of Thailand's total hill tribe population; many arrived from present-day Myanmar beginning in the 17th and 18th centuries and settled along the border provinces stretching from Mae Hong Son through Kanchanaburi ([Wikipedia — Hill tribe (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_tribe_(Thailand)); [Wikipedia — Peopling of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peopling_of_Thailand)). The **Hmong** (also called Meo), who migrated from southern China through Laos during the 19th century, form the second-largest group, with estimates ranging from about 125,000 to 200,000 people settled mainly around Chiang Mai and Chiang Rai ([Wikipedia — Peopling of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peopling_of_Thailand)). Smaller groups include the **Lahu** (roughly 52,000–100,000), **Akha** (around 60,000–70,000), **Lisu** (about 16,000–50,000), and **Mien/Yao** (around 40,000) ([Wikipedia — Peopling of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peopling_of_Thailand)). Many hill tribe members historically lacked Thai citizenship; a 2025 government initiative accelerated naturalization for nearly half a million stateless ethnic minority individuals, with Chiang Rai province — part of the Golden Triangle — recording the highest number of stateless residents ([Wikipedia — Hill tribe (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_tribe_(Thailand))).

## Smaller Communities and Recent Immigrants

Beyond these larger groups, Thailand hosts numerous smaller communities: Mon speakers (roughly 414,500) descended from one of Southeast Asia's oldest civilizations; Vietnamese, Indian, Pakistani, and Iranian minorities concentrated in urban centers; small Austronesian groups such as the Moken "sea gypsies" of the Andaman coast; and the Maniq, a Negrito hunter-gatherer group numbering only a few hundred people in the far south ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Thailand)). A significant modern migrant labor population from Myanmar, Laos, and Cambodia adds a further layer to the country's social fabric, even though these groups are not classified as indigenous ethnicities.
