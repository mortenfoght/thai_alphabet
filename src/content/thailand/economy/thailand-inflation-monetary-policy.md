# Inflation and Monetary Policy in Thailand

*How the Bank of Thailand is managing inflation and interest rates in 2025-2026, from near-deflation and rate cuts to a return toward the 1-3% target.*

Thailand's inflation and monetary policy have followed an unusual path over the past two years, swinging from near-deflationary territory in 2025 to a renewed pickup in prices by mid-2026. The Bank of Thailand (BOT), the country's central bank, has responded with a series of interest rate cuts aimed at supporting a sluggish economy without letting price stability slip. Understanding this dynamic — and the delicate balance the BOT is trying to strike — is essential to understanding Thailand's broader macroeconomic story heading into 2027.

## From Deflation Risk to a Rate-Cutting Cycle

For most of 2025, Thailand's headline inflation ran far below its official target. Between January and July 2024, headline inflation averaged just 0.11%, well under the Bank of Thailand's target range of 1.0% to 3.0% ([Market Research Thailand](https://marketresearchthailand.com/insights/articles/thailand-cbt-inflation-strategy-deflation-test)). By 2025, Thailand recorded three consecutive months of outright negative inflation — down 0.22% in April, 0.57% in May, and 0.25% in June — before deflation deepened further to -0.70% in July ([Market Research Thailand](https://marketresearchthailand.com/insights/articles/thailand-cbt-inflation-strategy-deflation-test); [UOB Group](https://www.uobgroup.com/assets/web-resources/research/pdf/MN_250807C.pdf)). Full-year 2025 headline inflation ultimately averaged -0.14%, according to Thailand's Ministry of Commerce, with December 2025 alone showing a 0.28% year-on-year decline ([Trade Policy and Strategy Office](https://uploads.tpso.go.th/editor/pdf/1767765216_103bc1b2d5372889ba4e.pdf); [Wikipedia — Economy of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Thailand)).

Crucially, core inflation — which strips out volatile fresh food and energy prices — stayed marginally positive throughout this period, averaging 0.84% for the year, indicating Thailand was experiencing disinflation driven largely by supply-side factors rather than a genuine deflationary spiral ([Bangkok Bank](https://www.bangkokbank.com/th-TH/-/media/bb74c892bae647178b0a0da747b327db.ashx); [Trade Policy and Strategy Office](https://uploads.tpso.go.th/editor/pdf/1767765216_103bc1b2d5372889ba4e.pdf)). The Bank of Thailand repeatedly emphasized that despite the negative headline figures, most goods and services continued rising or remained broadly stable in price, meaning deflation risk was limited even as the headline number flashed red ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/content/dam/bot/documents/en/our-roles/monetary-policy/mpc-publication/monetary-policy-report/MPR_2025_Q3.pdf)).

## Why Inflation Fell So Low

Three main forces drove the sustained low-inflation environment through 2025: declining global crude oil prices, government fuel subsidies at the retail pump, and lower raw food prices amid favorable agricultural supply conditions ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/content/dam/bot/documents/en/our-roles/monetary-policy/mpc-publication/monetary-policy-report/MPR_2025_Q3.pdf)). Thailand's slow economic recovery and soft domestic demand also kept underlying price pressures muted, reinforcing the disinflationary trend even as many other economies globally faced stickier inflation ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/content/dam/bot/documents/en/our-roles/monetary-policy/mpc-publication/monetary-policy-report/MPR_2025_Q3.pdf)).

## The Bank of Thailand's Response: Cutting Rates

Facing this weak inflation backdrop alongside slowing GDP growth, the Bank of Thailand's Monetary Policy Committee pursued a sustained easing cycle. The central bank cut its policy rate by 25 basis points in April 2025 — its third cut since October 2024 — bringing the rate down to 2.0% at the time ([Market Research Thailand](https://marketresearchthailand.com/insights/articles/thailand-cbt-inflation-strategy-deflation-test)). The committee held rates steady at 1.5% in October 2025, defying market expectations of a further cut, before resuming easing with a unanimous 25-basis-point cut in December 2025 that brought the policy rate to 1.25% ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/interest-rate/news/491257); [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thai-central-bank-cuts-policy-rate-by-25-bps-expected-2025-12-17/)).

The BOT then delivered a surprise cut in February 2026, lowering the policy rate to 1.00% — its lowest level since September 2022 — defying market expectations of a hold ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/thailand-surprises-with-rate-cut-to-bolster-fragile-recovery); [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/interest-rate/news/528288)). By April 2026, with growth concerns mounting due to Middle East-linked energy price shocks, the committee voted 6-0 to hold the rate steady at 1.00%, judging the setting appropriate to support a slowing economy while noting that inflation pressures were largely supply-driven rather than demand-driven ([Facebook — The Nation Thailand](https://www.facebook.com/TheNationThailand/posts/the-bank-of-thailands-mpc-voted-6-0-to-keep-the-policy-rate-at-10-on-april-29-ci/1396978439122153/)).

## The Inflation Target Framework

Thailand's monetary policy operates under an explicit inflation-targeting framework, with the Cabinet formally approving a headline inflation target of 1–3% for the medium term, a range that has remained unchanged from 2025 into 2026 ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/policy/40060565)). This band was reaffirmed in the joint agreement between the Ministry of Finance and the Bank of Thailand approved on December 30, 2025, with policymakers stating the range would guide inflation gradually back toward target while explicitly working to prevent deflation, defined as persistently negative, broad-based price declines ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/policy/40060565)).

## Inflation's Return in 2026

By the second quarter of 2026, the disinflationary picture had shifted markedly. The annual inflation rate climbed to 2.89% in April 2026 — the highest reading in more than three years — driven primarily by higher energy costs ([Business Times](https://www.businesstimes.com.sg/international/thailand-sees-2-growth-and-3-inflation-2026-central-bank-chief-says)). Bank of Thailand Governor Vitai Ratanakorn projected headline inflation would reach around 3% for full-year 2026 before easing to about 1.4–1.5% in 2027, with GDP growth expected around 2% in 2026 and 1.7% in 2027 ([Business Times](https://www.businesstimes.com.sg/international/thailand-sees-2-growth-and-3-inflation-2026-central-bank-chief-says); [Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/en/thai-economy.html)).

By June 2026, inflation had eased somewhat to 2.42% year-on-year — still within the 1–3% target band — as easing tensions in the Middle East and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz pushed global crude oil prices, and thus domestic energy costs, lower ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/inflation-cpi)). This marked the third consecutive month of positive inflation after Thailand emerged from a year-long deflationary streak, though the pace of increase was slowing ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/inflation-cpi)). Core inflation registered at a more modest 1.23% in June 2026, still comfortably below the upper end of the target band ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/en/thai-economy.html)).

## Balancing Growth Support and Price Stability

The Bank of Thailand's rate cuts through late 2025 and early 2026 reflect a central bank prioritizing growth support given a weakening economic backdrop — household debt, soft exports, and a struggling tourism sector — even as it monitored for any sign that ultra-low rates might eventually stoke unwanted inflation. As of mid-2026, that balancing act appeared to be paying off: inflation had returned to positive territory within the target band without spiraling, while growth, though still modest, had stabilized somewhat compared to the darkest projections of early 2026 ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/inflation-cpi); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40064844)). Policymakers now face the challenge of guiding inflation back toward its medium-term anchor near 2% while continuing to nurture a recovery that remains fragile relative to Thailand's pre-2015 growth trajectory.
