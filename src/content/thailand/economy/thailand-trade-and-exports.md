# Thailand's Trade and Exports Explained

*A clear guide to Thailand's trade and exports in 2025-2026, covering record export values, top trading partners, key products, and rising US tariff risks.*

Thailand's trade and exports sit at the heart of its economic model, with exports of goods and services historically accounting for well over half of GDP ([Wikipedia — Economy of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Thailand)). In 2025, Thailand posted a record US$339.6 billion in merchandise exports, an increase of 12.9% over the previous year, even as the country navigated new U.S. tariff measures and a widening trade imbalance with China ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061607)). Trade is not a peripheral feature of the Thai economy — it is the engine, and understanding its structure explains much of what drives Thailand's overall growth and vulnerability.

## Record Exports Amid Global Uncertainty

Thai exports reached US$339.64 billion in 2025 (11.14 trillion baht), marking an all-time high and the fastest annual growth rate in four years ([SCB EIC](https://www.scbeic.com/en/detail/product/10015)). December 2025 alone saw exports surge 16.8% year-on-year, the 18th consecutive month of growth, driven overwhelmingly by electronics and electrical appliances ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061607)). Imports grew at a nearly identical pace, up 12.9% to US$344.94 billion, leaving Thailand with an overall trade deficit of roughly US$5.31 billion for the year ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061607); [Thairath](https://en.thairath.co.th/money/experts_pool/columnist/2909718)).

Much of the export strength reflected front-loaded shipments as exporters rushed orders ahead of anticipated U.S. tariff increases, a pattern the World Bank expects to unwind through 2026, with merchandise export growth slowing notably and tariff-exposed sectors facing slight contraction while electronics continue outperforming ([Xinhua](https://english.news.cn/20260211/de9ac7ba843a4d20a6eb954e87da665c/c.html)).

## Thailand's Top Export Products

Electrical machinery and equipment led Thai exports in 2025 at US$63.2 billion, or 18.7% of the total, followed by machinery including computers at US$61.1 billion (18.1%), vehicles at US$34.2 billion (10.1%), gems and precious metals at US$26.6 billion (7.9%), and rubber products at US$20.6 billion (6.1%) ([World's Top Exports](https://www.worldstopexports.com/thailands-top-10-exports/)). Individually, the single highest-value export item was computers and optical readers at US$22.6 billion, up 50.9% year-on-year, followed by smartphones and other telephone devices at US$17.2 billion and unwrought gold at US$13.0 billion ([World's Top Exports](https://www.worldstopexports.com/thailands-top-10-exports/)).

Electronics have become the single most important growth driver, representing 21.5% of total Thai export value in 2025, up from 17.6% in 2024, fueled by demand for AI-related technology and expanding global investment in data centers ([SCB EIC](https://www.scbeic.com/en/detail/product/10015)). Green goods — including energy-efficient cooling technology, solar photovoltaic products, and EV-related components — now account for close to 10% of total exports, with Thailand holding close to a third of the global market for reverse-cycle air conditioners ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/thailand/publication/thailand-economic-monitor-february-2026-advanced-green-manufacturing-for-growth)).

On the agricultural side, Thailand remains a major exporter of rice, natural rubber, cassava, and fresh fruit, though 2025 was a weaker year for these categories, with agricultural and agro-industrial exports contracting slightly amid natural disasters and stiff global competition; rice exports specifically fell by double digits during parts of the year ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061607); [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-export-growth-slows-us-tariffs-bite-2025-09-24/)).

## Key Trading Partners

China, Japan, and the United States dominate Thailand's export destinations, though the relationships look starkly different depending on direction of trade. Thailand ran a substantial trade surplus with the United States of US$51.4 billion in 2025, up from US$35.6 billion the year before, as exports to the US — particularly electronics — surged 54.3% in December alone ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/thailand-s-trade-data-point-to-tariff-bypassing-shipments); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061638)). Total two-way trade with the US reached US$93.65 billion in 2025 ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40061638)).

The relationship with China runs in the opposite direction. Thailand's trade deficit with China widened to US$67.9 billion in 2025, up from US$45.3 billion in 2024, as Thai imports of Chinese goods — including machinery, electronics, and consumer products — continued to outpace Thai exports to China ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/thailand-s-trade-data-point-to-tariff-bypassing-shipments)). China nonetheless remains Thailand's largest single export destination by volume in some trade datasets, purchasing roughly US$108.8 billion of Thai goods in 2025, ahead of Japan at US$30.0 billion and Taiwan at US$25.8 billion ([TradeInt](https://www.tradeint.com/product/countries-covered/asia-import-export-trade-data/thailand-trade-data/?year=2025&flow=export&depth=hs6)).

## The US Tariff Challenge

The most significant trade policy risk facing Thailand is the reciprocal tariff regime introduced by the United States in 2025. Under the framework agreement reached in October 2025, Thailand pledged to reduce its trade surplus with the United States by 70% within three to five years ([KPMG](https://kpmg.com/th/en/insights/2025/08/th-tax-news-flash-issue-155.html)). The US Trade Representative's office notes that the United States runs its 11th-largest goods trade deficit globally with Thailand ([USTR](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/fact-sheets/2025/october/fact-sheet-united-states-and-thailand-reach-framework-agreement-reciprocal-trade)). Thailand's effective US tariff rate stood at roughly 17.3%, comparatively favorable versus some regional competitors, helping electronics and AI-related component exports remain resilient even as tariff-exposed sectors like textiles, furniture, and steel products faced sharper cost pressures ([World Bank](https://thedocs.worldbank.org/en/doc/c6aceb75bed03729ef4ff9404dd7f125-0500012021/related/mpo-tha.pdf); [LH Bank](https://www.lhbank.co.th/getattachment/b0398ea4-2823-4810-b8ff-277a2e42723e/economic-analysis-Economic-and-Industry-Analysis-2025-Reciprocal-Tariff-Impact)).

Bank of Thailand analysis modeled a scenario in which full reciprocal tariffs could cut Thai export value by 8.3%, shaving a full percentage point off GDP growth, with agro-manufacturing and electrical appliance exporters most exposed given their heavy reliance on the US market ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/content/dam/bot/documents/en/our-roles/monetary-policy/mpc-publication/monetary-policy-report/mpr-box/MPR_2025_Q1_BOX1.pdf)). Separately, some analysts have flagged a pattern of Chinese-origin goods being routed through Thailand to bypass direct US tariffs on China, a dynamic regulators in Washington continue to scrutinize ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/thailand-s-trade-data-point-to-tariff-bypassing-shipments)).

## Border Trade and Regional Links

Beyond seaborne and air trade, Thailand also maintains substantial land-based commerce with its immediate neighbors. Malaysia stands as Thailand's leading border trading partner, followed by Laos, Myanmar, and Cambodia, with border and cross-border trade generating a healthy surplus driven largely by diesel, processed petroleum, and cross-border electricity and natural gas flows ([Royal Thai Government](https://thailand.prd.go.th/en/content/category/detail/id/2078/iid/473106)).

## Outlook for Thai Trade

Thailand's export engine enters 2026 facing a more difficult environment than the record-breaking headline numbers of 2025 suggest. The World Bank expects merchandise export growth to slow notably as the front-loading effect fades and US tariff impacts phase in more fully, even as electronics continues to perform well and green manufacturing offers a new avenue for diversification ([Xinhua](https://english.news.cn/20260211/de9ac7ba843a4d20a6eb954e87da665c/c.html); [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/thailand/publication/thailand-economic-monitor-february-2026-advanced-green-manufacturing-for-growth)). How Thailand balances its surplus with Washington against its deepening dependence on Chinese imports will likely shape trade policy debates well into 2027.
