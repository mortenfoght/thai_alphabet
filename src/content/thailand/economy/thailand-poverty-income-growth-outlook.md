# Poverty, Income Status, and Thailand's Growth Outlook

*A data-driven look at Thailand's poverty rate, upper-middle-income status, household debt, and whether the country can still reach high-income status by 2037.*

Thailand's poverty rate has fallen dramatically over the past quarter-century, yet the country's broader growth outlook now raises serious doubts about whether it can complete the final climb to high-income status. The national poverty rate dropped from 42.5% in 2000 to just 6.3% in 2021 ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/country/thailand)), one of the more striking development success stories in Southeast Asia. But Thailand's growth has slowed so much in recent years that its long-standing ambition of reaching high-income status by 2037 now appears increasingly out of reach ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/country/thailand)).

## Thailand's Remarkable Poverty Reduction

Thailand's progress on poverty reduction ranks among the most significant in the region. Using the national poverty line, the poverty rate fell from around 65% in 1988 to below 9% by the mid-2010s, and further still to roughly 5.4% by 2022 ([Wikipedia — Economy of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Thailand); [World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099503004222538328/pdf/IDU-8b60dad6-f5b6-40db-a62e-73dfeac4d790.pdf)). Using the World Bank's international poverty line of $3.00 a day (2021 PPP), extreme poverty in Thailand is now effectively negligible ([World Bank Open Data](https://data.worldbank.org/?locations=TH-XO-XD)).

Measured against the World Bank's upper-middle-income poverty line — recently updated to $8.30 per person per day in 2021 PPP terms — roughly 9.9% to 10.1% of Thailand's population was classified as poor in 2023, down sharply from 16.9% in 2015 ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099503004222538328/pdf/IDU-8b60dad6-f5b6-40db-a62e-73dfeac4d790.pdf); [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/country/thailand)). Progress has nonetheless slowed markedly since 2015 compared with the pace of the two prior decades, and poverty briefly ticked up during the COVID-19 pandemic, rising to 6.4% (national line) in 2020 before resuming its decline ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/780771636649923876/pdf/Thailand-Poverty-Brief.pdf)).

## Who Remains in Poverty

World Bank research on Thailand's remaining poor population finds that about 35% of poor household heads are out of the labor force entirely, a rate 11 percentage points higher than for middle-class households ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/780771636649923876/pdf/Thailand-Poverty-Brief.pdf)). Among those in the labor market, the poor are predominantly self-employed (64%) and concentrated in agriculture (72%), reflecting the persistent income gap between Thailand's urbanized, industrialized economy and its still-large rural, farming population ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/780771636649923876/pdf/Thailand-Poverty-Brief.pdf)). Labor income represents only about 28% of poor households' average income, with public assistance and remittances filling much of the remaining gap ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/780771636649923876/pdf/Thailand-Poverty-Brief.pdf)).

## Income Inequality Remains High

Despite falling poverty, income inequality in Thailand remains elevated by international standards. The Gini index — a standard measure of income inequality — stood around 34.8 to 35.1 in the early 2020s ([World Bank](https://databankfiles.worldbank.org/public/ddpext_download/poverty/987B9C90-CB9F-4D93-AE8C-750588BF00QA/current/Global_POVEQ_THA.pdf)). More strikingly, the richest 10% of the population captured 48.8% of net personal income in 2021, one of the highest concentrations among countries with comparable data ([EABC Thailand](https://www.eabc-thailand.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/2025-EABC-Position-Paper-Introduction-G.-Income-gap-and-Wealth-gap2.pdf)). Social assistance programs have expanded significantly, with about 71% of the population receiving some form of government transfer by 2021, though wealthier households often received larger transfers in absolute terms than the poorest ([EABC Thailand](https://www.eabc-thailand.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/2025-EABC-Position-Paper-Introduction-G.-Income-gap-and-Wealth-gap2.pdf)).

## Upper-Middle-Income Status Since 2011

Thailand reached upper-middle-income status in 2011, a designation based on gross national income per capita thresholds set by the World Bank ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/country/thailand)). GDP per capita has continued to climb since, reaching approximately US$8,057 in 2025 and projected to edge up to around US$8,105–8,110 in 2026 ([World Bank](https://data.worldbank.org/country/thailand); [Wikipedia — Economy of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Thailand)). That places Thailand firmly in the upper-middle-income bracket but still meaningfully below the threshold for high-income classification.

## Why the Growth Slowdown Threatens the 2037 Target

Thailand's government has long targeted high-income status by 2037, but persistent low growth has cast serious doubt on that timeline. GDP growth registered just 2.5% in 2024, and the World Bank now projects growth slowing further to around 1.3–1.8% in 2025–2026, well below the pace needed for Thailand to close the income gap with high-income economies on schedule ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/country/thailand); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40064844)). The World Bank's April 2026 assessment explicitly noted that Thailand has been growing at just 1–2% annually in recent years, a structural pace that stands in sharp contrast to faster-growing regional peers ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40064844)).

In June 2026, Thailand's government set a new 12-year goal to reach high-income status, an implicit acknowledgment that the original 2037 target was slipping out of reach and needed to be reframed with a fresh timeline ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/business/economy/40067724)).

## Household Debt as a Structural Drag

A major headwind clouding both the poverty and growth outlook is Thailand's elevated household debt burden. High household debt levels and tight credit conditions have been repeatedly cited by the World Bank, the IMF, and the Bank of Thailand as key constraints on domestic consumption, limiting the extent to which improving incomes translate into broader economic momentum ([World Bank](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099021126000575478/txt/P513268-05951948-1534-41bc-87c7-59ff89523a00.txt)). Thailand's central bank governor indicated in late 2025 that debt-relief measures were being planned to help address the burden weighing on households and, by extension, the broader economy ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thai-economy-improve-early-next-year-debt-measures-planned-central-bank-chief-2025-10-28/)).

## The Path Forward

Thailand's poverty-reduction achievements over the past three decades are undeniable, but the country now faces a more complex challenge: sustaining income growth broadly enough, and fast enough, to advance beyond upper-middle-income status. With GDP growth stuck in the 1–2% range through much of 2025 and 2026, elevated household debt constraining consumption, and income inequality still comparatively high, Thailand's economic policymakers face a narrowing window to hit ambitious income-status targets — a challenge that now spans not just poverty alleviation, but the harder task of durable, broad-based growth.
