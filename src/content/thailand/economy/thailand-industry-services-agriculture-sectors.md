# Industry, Services, and Agriculture: A Sector Breakdown of Thailand's Economy

*A detailed breakdown of Thailand's economy by sector, covering manufacturing, services, and agriculture's shrinking but still vital role in national output.*

Thailand's economy sector breakdown reveals a country that has moved decisively from farm to factory to service counter over the past six decades. Services now generate the largest share of Thailand's roughly US$577 billion GDP, industry contributes a substantial secondary layer built around automotive and electronics manufacturing, and agriculture — while a shrinking slice of output — still employs a disproportionately large share of the national workforce ([Wikipedia — Economy of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Thailand)). Each sector tells a different story about how Thailand's economy actually functions, and where its vulnerabilities lie.

## Services: The Largest Slice of Output

Services dominate the modern Thai economy, accounting for roughly 58–63% of GDP depending on the measurement source and year ([Statista](https://www.statista.com/statistics/331893/share-of-economic-sectors-in-the-gdp-in-thailand/); [SECO](https://www.seco.admin.ch/dam/seco/en/dokumente/Aussenwirtschaft/Wirtschaftsbeziehungen/L%C3%A4nderinformationen/Asien_Ozeanien/wirtschaftsbericht_thailand.pdf.download.pdf/Wirtschaftsbericht_Thailand_2025.pdf)). Within services, wholesale and retail trade is the single largest contributor, followed by accommodation and food services, transport and storage, financial and insurance activities, and information and communication ([the-shiv.com](https://the-shiv.com/thailand-economy/)). According to Thailand's 2024 GDP breakdown, wholesale and retail trade alone represented nearly 16% of GDP, while accommodation and food services — closely tied to tourism — contributed about 5.9% ([the-shiv.com](https://the-shiv.com/thailand-economy/)).

The services sector has also proven the most consistent growth engine in recent years. It expanded 3.9% in 2024 and 3.3% over the first three quarters of 2025, driven by wholesale and retail trade, accommodation, food services, and transport ([AMRO](https://amro-asia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Thailand-Annual-Consultation-Report-2025_for-publication.pdf)). However, growth within the tourism-linked accommodation and food services subsector decelerated sharply, from 9.6% in 2024 to just 3.3% in the first three quarters of 2025, as the slowdown in international tourist arrivals rippled through the broader services economy ([AMRO](https://amro-asia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Thailand-Annual-Consultation-Report-2025_for-publication.pdf)).

## Industry: Manufacturing's Central Role

Industry — dominated by manufacturing, alongside mining, utilities, and construction — contributes roughly 30–34% of Thailand's GDP ([Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/thailand/gdp-growth); [SECO](https://www.seco.admin.ch/dam/seco/en/dokumente/Aussenwirtschaft/Wirtschaftsbeziehungen/L%C3%A4nderinformationen/Asien_Ozeanien/wirtschaftsbericht_thailand.pdf.download.pdf/Wirtschaftsbericht_Thailand_2025.pdf)). Manufacturing alone typically accounts for around 24–34% of output depending on methodology, employing close to 10% of the national workforce and concentrated heavily in industrial estates across Central and Eastern Thailand ([Market Research Thailand](https://marketresearchthailand.com/insights/articles/how-thailand-electronics-manufacturing-is-winning-the-china-plus-one-shift)).

### Automotive Manufacturing

Thailand has long been dubbed the "Detroit of Asia" for its outsized automotive manufacturing base. The automotive sector accounts for roughly 3.1% of GDP directly and employs more than 570,000 workers, while vehicle exports reached US$34.2 billion in 2025 — about 10.1% of total exports ([World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/thailand/publication/thailand-economic-monitor-february-2026-advanced-green-manufacturing-for-growth); [World's Top Exports](https://www.worldstopexports.com/thailands-top-10-exports/)). The sector historically contributed over 10% of GDP when counting its full supply chain, though its growth contribution has moderated as the industry transitions toward electric vehicles, a shift analysts describe as creating short-term "transitional frictions" even as more than 80% of existing auto-parts production can be adapted for EVs with limited modification ([AMRO](https://amro-asia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Selected-Issue_Thailands-Automotive-Sector-at-a-Crossroads_Managing-the-Transition-to-EVs.pdf); [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/thailand/publication/thailand-economic-monitor-february-2026-advanced-green-manufacturing-for-growth)).

### Electronics and the AI Supply Chain

Electronics has emerged as Thailand's fastest-growing industrial segment, propelled by global demand for AI-related hardware and data center components. Electronics exports grew 38.3% for the full year 2025 and represented 25% of total export value, with December 2025 shipments to the United States alone surging 52.8% ([Thairath](https://en.thairath.co.th/money/experts_pool/columnist/2909718)). Mordor Intelligence estimates Thailand's electronics manufacturing services market reached roughly US$6.88 billion in 2025, projected to climb to US$12.57 billion by 2031 ([Market Research Thailand](https://marketresearchthailand.com/insights/articles/how-thailand-electronics-manufacturing-is-winning-the-china-plus-one-shift)). Manufacturing overall remained sluggish in 2024 and much of 2025, contracting slightly even as high-end export producers like computer and canned seafood makers showed resilience ([AMRO](https://amro-asia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Thailand-Annual-Consultation-Report-2025_for-publication.pdf)).

## Agriculture: Shrinking Share, Enduring Employment

Agriculture's contribution to Thai GDP has fallen dramatically from close to 40% in the early 1960s to somewhere between 6% and 9% today, depending on the source and year measured ([OpenEdition Books](https://books.openedition.org/irdeditions/32537?lang=fr); [SECO](https://www.seco.admin.ch/dam/seco/en/dokumente/Aussenwirtschaft/Wirtschaftsbeziehungen/L%C3%A4nderinformationen/Asien_Ozeanien/wirtschaftsbericht_thailand.pdf.download.pdf/Wirtschaftsbericht_Thailand_2025.pdf)). Yet the sector remains socio-politically vital: it still employs roughly 30% of Thailand's total labor force, around 12.7 million workers ([Bank of Thailand](https://www.bot.or.th/content/dam/bot/financial-innovation/sustainable-finance/green/taxonomy/03_EN_Thailand_Taxonomy-Agriculture_Sector.pdf)). That mismatch between agriculture's small GDP share and its large employment footprint underscores persistently low productivity and income levels among rural farming households compared with urban and industrial workers.

Rice remains the most iconic Thai agricultural export, with an estimated 16 million of Thailand's 40% agricultural workforce classified specifically as rice farmers ([ASEAN Access](https://www.aseanaccess.com/images/pdf/agriculture/THAILAND_AGRI_update.pdf)). Thailand is also the world's leading exporter of natural rubber and the top global exporter of fresh fruit, alongside significant rubber, sugar, cassava, and livestock production ([DHL](https://www.dhl.com/discover/en-th/b2b-advice/b2b-e-commerce-advice/top-exports-from-thailand-2025); [National Statistical Office](https://www.nso.go.th/public/e-book/Statistical-Yearbook/SYB-2025/16/)). However, rubber's share of agricultural exports has fallen sharply, from 36.3% in 2013 to just 16.4% in 2024, reflecting price volatility and diversification pressure within the sector ([Bangkok Bank](https://www.bangkokbank.com/th-TH/-/media/844e9bc0a2954b40b70372fe4ea84edb.ashx)).

Agricultural output has swung sharply in recent years: it contracted 1.1% in 2024 before rebounding 5.1% over the first three quarters of 2025, aided by favorable weather and improved crop yields ([AMRO](https://amro-asia.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Thailand-Annual-Consultation-Report-2025_for-publication.pdf)). First-quarter 2025 data from Thailand's National Economic and Social Development Council showed agriculture surging 5.7%, driven by higher yields of paddy, sugarcane, rubber, fruits, and poultry, even as cassava, oil palm, and fishery output declined ([NESDC](https://www.nesdc.go.th/download/gross-domestic-product-q3-2025/?ddl=b057f124d1b00daa0d72e4b1f4120231)).

## Why the Sector Mix Matters

The imbalance between agriculture's employment share and its GDP contribution remains one of Thailand's core structural challenges, contributing to rural-urban income gaps and vulnerability to weather shocks and global commodity price swings. Meanwhile, the pivot toward electronics, green manufacturing, and higher-value services suggests where future growth is most likely to originate — even as traditional pillars like automotive manufacturing and tourism-linked services navigate their own transitions. Understanding this three-part structure — services-led, industry-supported, agriculture-anchored in employment if not output — is essential to grasping how Thailand's economy actually moves.
