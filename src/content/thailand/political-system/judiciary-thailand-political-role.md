# Thailand's Judiciary and Its Outsized Role in Politics

*Why Thailand's judiciary, especially the Constitutional Court, has become a dominant political force—removing prime ministers and dissolving major parties.*

Thailand's judiciary is formally independent of the executive and legislative branches, but it has become one of the most consequential political actors in the country — routinely removing prime ministers, dissolving political parties, and reshaping election outcomes in ways that elected legislatures cannot easily reverse ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Understanding modern Thai politics is difficult without understanding how this judicial power works and why it has expanded so dramatically since the 2000s.

## Structure of the Thai Judiciary

Thailand's judicial power is exercised through four separate bodies operating under the 2017 constitution: the Constitutional Court, the Court of Justice (which includes the Supreme Court), the Administrative Court (topped by the Supreme Administrative Court), and the Military Court ([Court of Justice of Thailand](https://www.coj.go.th/th/content/page/index/id/91994)). Each has a distinct jurisdiction:

### The Supreme Court
The Supreme Court, currently led by Chief Judge Adisak Tantiwong, sits atop the ordinary court system and hears cases specified by the constitution or statute, as well as appeals from lower and appellate courts ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Court of Justice of Thailand](https://www.coj.go.th/th/content/page/index/id/91994)). It has also handled major corruption cases against political figures, including proceedings tied to former prime minister Thaksin Shinawatra.

### The Supreme Administrative Court
Led by Chief Judge Prasitsak Meelarp, this court and its regional counterparts adjudicate disputes between individuals and state agencies, including cases over government contracts, land disputes, and administrative decisions that touch on national sovereignty issues, such as a past ruling connected to the Preah Vihear temple dispute with Cambodia ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Administrative Court of Thailand](https://www.admincourt.go.th/adminCourt/en/structure.php)).

### The Constitutional Court
Chaired by Chief Judge Nakarin Mektrairat, the nine-member Constitutional Court reviews the constitutionality of legislation and verifies the qualifications of members of the House of Representatives, senators, and ministers ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Court of Justice of Thailand](https://www.coj.go.th/th/content/page/index/id/91994)). This is, by far, the body with the greatest direct impact on day-to-day politics.

## The Constitutional Court's Political Reach

The Constitutional Court was created for the first time under the 1997 "people's constitution," alongside the Administrative Court and the Ombudsman, as part of a broader effort to build institutional checks on executive power ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Ironically, these very institutions "later became a threat to politicians," in the words of Wikipedia's account of Thai politics, particularly once the financial dealings of populist prime minister Thaksin Shinawatra became a national controversy in the mid-2000s ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

Since then, the court has repeatedly intervened at pivotal moments:

- In 2007, the Constitutional Court dissolved Thaksin's Thai Rak Thai Party and banned 111 of its members from politics for five years, following the 2006 coup that first ousted him ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).
- In August 2024, the court removed sitting Pheu Thai prime minister Srettha Thavisin over an ethics violation ([Asialink](https://asialink.unimelb.edu.au/diplomacy/insights/will-thailand-become-more-democratic-after-vote/)).
- Days later, in the same month, the court dissolved the Move Forward Party — winner of the most seats in the 2023 general election — and banned eleven of its executives from politics for ten years, ruling that its campaign to reform the lèse-majesté law posed a threat to the constitutional monarchy ([Human Rights Watch](https://www.hrw.org/news/2024/08/07/thailand-constitutional-court-dissolves-opposition-party)).
- In August 2025, the court removed Srettha's successor, Prime Minister Paetongtarn Shinawatra, over a leaked diplomatic phone call with Cambodian leader Hun Sen amid a border dispute, finding she had violated standards of evident dishonesty and serious ethical conduct under Sections 106(4) and 106(5) of the constitution ([Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/thailand-constitutional-court-prime-minister/); [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thai-prime-minister-removed-by-court-triggering-power-scramble-2025-08-29/)).

In the Paetongtarn case, the complaint that triggered her removal was filed by a group of conservative senators — illustrating how the appointed and indirectly elected Senate can work in tandem with the courts to check elected governments ([Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/thailand-constitutional-court-prime-minister/)).

## Why the Judiciary Has Grown So Powerful

Legal scholars have described this pattern as "blurring the divide between legal and political liability," since the court's rulings often turn on broadly worded ethical standards that give justices significant discretion over what counts as a disqualifying offense ([Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/thailand-constitutional-court-prime-minister/)). The 2017 constitution, drafted under military oversight after the 2014 coup, embedded a wide array of unelected veto points across the political system — including the courts, the Senate, and various appointed oversight bodies — that collectively shape government formation, discipline elected officials, and raise the bar for constitutional change ([Asialink](https://asialink.unimelb.edu.au/diplomacy/insights/will-thailand-become-more-democratic-after-vote/)).

Human rights organizations and foreign governments have criticized several of these rulings as disproportionate. The United Nations human rights chief said he was "deeply troubled" by the Move Forward Party's dissolution, while Amnesty International called it an "untenable decision that stifles human rights" ([UN News](https://news.un.org/en/story/2024/08/1152976); [Amnesty International](https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2024/08/thailand-dissolution-of-move-forward-party-an-untenable-decision-that-stifles-human-rights/)). Yet the court's decisions have consistently stood, since Thailand's institutional design gives it no effective avenue for legislative or popular override.

## Corruption, Enforcement, and Public Trust

Beyond high-profile political cases, corruption remains a persistent challenge across the wider justice and administrative system. Transparency International's 2018 Corruption Perceptions Index ranked Thailand 99th out of the countries surveyed, and researchers estimated that bureaucratic corruption alone cost the country up to 100 billion baht that year ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Successive governments, including military juntas that seized power partly on anti-corruption platforms, have struggled to shake this reputation, and some juntas became embroiled in corruption scandals of their own ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

## A Judiciary That Outlasts Governments

Perhaps the clearest measure of the judiciary's political weight is its durability. Elected governments in Thailand have come and gone through elections, coalitions, coups, and court rulings, but the constitutional and administrative court system has remained a constant fixture even through periods of martial law, continuing to operate — as it did after the 2014 coup — even when the constitution itself was suspended ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). With Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul now governing after two of his predecessors were removed by the Constitutional Court within thirteen months, the judiciary's role as the ultimate arbiter of who governs Thailand shows no sign of diminishing ([Asialink](https://asialink.unimelb.edu.au/diplomacy/insights/will-thailand-become-more-democratic-after-vote/)).
