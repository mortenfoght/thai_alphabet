# Political Parties and Movements in Thailand

*A look at Thailand's major political parties and street movements — Bhumjaithai, Pheu Thai, the People's Party, and the Red and Yellow Shirts.*

Political parties in Thailand have long operated in the shadow of street movements, royal politics, and repeated military intervention, producing a party landscape that shifts dramatically from one election to the next. The February 2026 general election underscored just how fluid this landscape remains: the conservative Bhumjaithai Party surged to a dominant first-place finish, the progressive People's Party held its urban base but lost ground nationally, and Pheu Thai — the Shinawatra family's longtime vehicle — collapsed to third place for the first time in over two decades ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c5y6534y3y5o)).

## The Current Party Landscape

### Bhumjaithai Party
Led by Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul since 2012, Bhumjaithai is a conservative, populist party whose base is concentrated in Buriram province and the surrounding northeast, tied closely to the Chidchob political family ([Wikipedia — Bhumjaithai Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhumjaithai_Party); [Khaosod English](https://www.khaosodenglish.com/featured/2024/06/29/new-thai-senators-a-new-power-base-not-red-or-orange-but-blue/)). The party enjoys support from Thailand's royalist conservative establishment and campaigned in 2026 heavily on nationalism amid ongoing tension with Cambodia, more than doubling its seat count to roughly 191–193 seats in the 500-member House of Representatives ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/6/thailand-election-2026-what-are-the-main-parties-what-do-polls-suggest); [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-pm-anutin-staked-his-election-nationalism-won-2026-02-08/)).

### People's Party (formerly Move Forward)
The People's Party is the direct successor to the Move Forward Party, which won the most seats of any party in the 2023 general election on a platform of monarchy and military reform but was blocked from forming a government by a Senate still under junta influence ([TIME](https://time.com/7314811/anutin-charnvirakul-thailand-prime-minister-bhumjaithai-pheu-thai-peoples-party/)). After the Constitutional Court dissolved Move Forward in August 2024 and banned eleven of its executives from politics for ten years over its stance on the lèse-majesté law, 143 of its lawmakers regrouped within 60 days under the new People's Party banner, led by Natthaphong Ruengpanyawut ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/8/9/thailands-disbanded-opposition-party-relaunches-under-new-name-leader); [The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2024/08/disbanded-thai-opposition-party-rebrands-as-peoples-party/)). In the 2026 election, the party swept all Bangkok constituencies but slipped to roughly 118–120 seats overall, down from Move Forward's 151 in 2023, and it has chosen to remain in opposition rather than join Anutin's coalition ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c5y6534y3y5o); [Wikipedia — People's Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Party_(Thailand))).

### Pheu Thai Party
Pheu Thai is the latest incarnation of the political network built by Thaksin Shinawatra, following earlier iterations Thai Rak Thai and the People's Power Party, both dissolved by court order ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Once Thailand's dominant electoral force — it won 265 of 500 seats in 2011 under Yingluck Shinawatra — Pheu Thai has declined sharply amid the removals of two of its prime ministers by the Constitutional Court and Thaksin's own imprisonment, falling to third place with about 74 seats in 2026 and losing every constituency in Chiang Mai, the Shinawatra family's ancestral stronghold, for the first time ever ([CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/16/asia/thailand-election-anutin-thaksin-intl-hnk); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/politics/40062301)). Despite the setback, Pheu Thai joined Anutin's governing coalition as a junior partner, securing around nine cabinet posts ([Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thailands-king-endorses-anutins-new-cabinet-royal-gazette-says)).

### Smaller and legacy parties
Other parties with parliamentary representation after 2026 include Kla Tham (roughly 58 seats, a newer conservative party that emerged as a surprise contender), the Democrat Party (around 21–22 seats, Thailand's oldest active party, led historically by figures like Chuan Leekpai and Abhisit Vejjajiva), Palang Pracharath (about 5 seats, a military-linked party once led by 2014 coup leader Prayut Chan-o-cha's allies), and the Prachachat Party, which represents Malay-Muslim minority interests in the southern border provinces ([Wikipedia — List of political parties in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Thailand); [Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thai-poll-body-certifies-election-results-clearing-path-for-new-parliament)).

## Street Movements That Shaped the Party System

Thai party politics cannot be separated from the mass movements that have repeatedly spilled onto Bangkok's streets.

### Red Shirts
The Red Shirts emerged as supporters of Thaksin Shinawatra after his ouster in the 2006 coup, drawing heavily on rural and northeastern voters who benefited from his populist policies, including debt relief for farmers and a low-cost universal healthcare scheme ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). The movement later rallied behind Thaksin's sister, Yingluck Shinawatra, and generally frames urban and military elite control of politics as a threat to democratic rule by the rural majority ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

### Yellow Shirts
The Yellow Shirts, formally the People's Alliance for Democracy, represent an opposing coalition of royalists, ultra-nationalists, and urban middle-class Thais who mobilized against Thaksin, accusing him of vote-buying, corruption, and authoritarian tendencies ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Led by media tycoon Sondhi Limthongkul, the movement's street protests set the stage for the 2006 coup and later occupied Bangkok's Government House and international airports in 2008, paralyzing parts of the country ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

### The 2020–2021 pro-democracy youth protests
A newer generation of protesters, largely students and young people, took to the streets from 2020 to 2021 demanding constitutional reform, the resignation of the Prayut government, and — unusually for Thailand — direct reform of the monarchy's role and the lèse-majesté law ([Wikipedia — Lèse-majesté in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A8se-majest%C3%A9_in_Thailand)). This movement fed directly into the rise of the Move Forward Party and its 2023 electoral breakthrough, and continues to shape the People's Party's platform and its uneasy relationship with more establishment-aligned institutions.

## A Recurring Pattern: Dissolution and Rebirth

One of the most distinctive features of the Thai party system is the frequency with which successful parties are dissolved by court order, only to reconstitute under a new name with much of the same leadership and voter base. Thai Rak Thai became the People's Power Party, which became Pheu Thai; Future Forward became Move Forward, which became the People's Party ([Wikipedia — Template:Thai political parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template:Thai_political_parties)). This cycle reflects the deep structural tension between Thailand's periodically dominant electoral coalitions and the judicial and military-linked institutions empowered to check them.

## Why the Party System Keeps Shifting

Money, patronage networks, and regional strongholds remain decisive in Thai elections, but 2026 demonstrated that even entrenched dynasties are not immune to rapid decline — Pheu Thai's collapse and Bhumjaithai's rise both occurred within a single electoral cycle ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/politics/40062301)). With the People's Party remaining the largest opposition bloc and controlling all of Bangkok, and with smaller parties like Kla Tham emerging as new power brokers, the coalition arithmetic that keeps Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul in office remains a live and closely watched negotiation ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cx2jn4z4eq0o)).
