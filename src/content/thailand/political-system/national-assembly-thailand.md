# The National Assembly of Thailand: House of Representatives and Senate

*How Thailand's National Assembly works: the 500-seat House of Representatives, the indirectly chosen Senate, and their roles in law and government.*

The National Assembly of Thailand is the kingdom's bicameral legislature, made up of the House of Representatives and the Senate, meeting at the Sappaya-Sapasathan complex in Bangkok ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). While the two chambers work together on lawmaking, they are chosen through completely different processes and carry very different political weight — a structural quirk rooted in the military-drafted 2017 constitution that continues to shape how power is distributed in Thai politics.

## The House of Representatives

The House of Representatives is the lower and more powerful chamber, with 500 members serving four-year terms ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Members are elected through a mixed system: 400 seats are filled on a constituency basis under first-past-the-post voting, while the remaining 100 are allocated through party-list proportional representation based on each party's national vote share ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/6/thailand-election-2026-what-are-the-main-parties-what-do-polls-suggest)). This ratio has shifted over successive constitutions — the 2019 election under the original 2017 rules used a 350/150 constituency-to-list split, while the 2023 and 2026 elections used a revised 400/100 split ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Wikipedia — 2026 Thai general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Thai_general_election)).

The House holds the decisive levers of executive accountability: it nominates the prime minister for royal appointment, and under Section 151 of the constitution, at least one-fifth of its members can initiate a no-confidence motion against individual ministers or the cabinet as a whole ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Sophon Saram currently serves as Speaker of the House, having been elected to the role in March 2026 following that year's general election ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Bhumjaithai Party — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhumjaithai_Party)).

### The 2026 election results

Thailand's most recent general election, held February 8, 2026, alongside a referendum on drafting a new constitution, produced a decisive result: the Bhumjaithai Party won roughly 191–193 seats, the People's Party (formerly Move Forward) took about 118–120 seats, Pheu Thai fell to third place with around 74 seats, and the newly formed Kla Tham Party won about 58 seats ([Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thai-poll-body-certifies-election-results-clearing-path-for-new-parliament); [Wikipedia — 2026 Thai general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Thai_general_election)). Turnout was about 71%, with roughly 37.8 million ballots cast ([Xinhua](https://english.news.cn/asiapacific/20260304/6b5e87d42e2a41ec80f1279bf60d3716/c.html)). The result marked a historic reversal for Pheu Thai, the Shinawatra family's political vehicle, which lost every constituency seat in Chiang Mai — its traditional stronghold — for the first time ([CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/16/asia/thailand-election-anutin-thaksin-intl-hnk)).

## The Senate: A Very Different Chamber

The Senate is the upper house, currently composed of 200 members serving five-year, non-renewable terms ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [IPU Parline](https://data.ipu.org/parliament/TH/TH-UC01/election/TH-UC01-E20240626)). Unlike the House, senators are not elected by the general public. Under Section 107 of the 2017 constitution, they are chosen through an indirect, multi-stage process among candidates from 20 professional and social groups — including law and justice, agriculture, public health, education, and business — designed to produce a chamber of subject-matter specialists rather than party politicians ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand); [Benar News](https://www.benarnews.org/english/news/thai/new-senate-07102024150651.html)).

This system replaced a 250-member Senate that had been directly appointed by the military junta following the 2014 coup — a body that controversially held the power to vote alongside the House on selecting the prime minister, and which used that power in 2019 to help keep coup leader Prayut Chan-o-cha in office ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). The junta-appointed Senate's term expired in May 2024, and the first elections under the new indirect system ran from June 9 to June 26, 2024, at district, provincial, and national levels, with roughly 48,000 candidates competing for the 200 seats ([Benar News](https://www.benarnews.org/english/news/thai/new-senate-07102024150651.html)). Mongkol Surasajja currently serves as President of the Senate ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

### Diminished but not powerless

Critically, the new Senate lost the outgoing chamber's power to vote for prime minister — a change welcomed by pro-democracy advocates as ending a key piece of the 2014 coup's institutional legacy ([Benar News](https://www.benarnews.org/english/news/thai/new-senate-07102024150651.html)). Yet the Senate retains real influence: it scrutinizes and can block ordinary legislation, votes on proposed constitutional amendments, and plays a role in confirming appointees to key independent oversight agencies, including bodies that oversee elections and anti-corruption enforcement ([IDEA Democracy Tracker](https://www.idea.int/democracytracker/report/thailand/july-2024)).

The 2024 Senate election also drew serious controversy. Observers and journalists noted that more than half of the elected senators appeared linked to the Bhumjaithai Party, with many candidates connected to Buriram province and the Chidchob family that founded the party ([Khaosod English](https://www.khaosodenglish.com/featured/2024/06/29/new-thai-senators-a-new-power-base-not-red-or-orange-but-blue/)). By mid-2025, roughly 100 of the 200 senators faced formal accusations of collusion and vote-fixing during the selection process, prompting investigations by the Election Commission and the Department of Special Investigation ([Wikipedia — 2025 Thai political crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Thai_political_crisis)).

## Voting Rights and Eligibility

Thai citizens qualify to vote in House elections under Section 95 of the 2017 constitution if they hold Thai nationality (naturalized citizens must have held it for at least five years), are at least 18 years old on election day, and have been registered in their constituency's household register for at least 90 days before the vote ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Ordinary citizens have no direct vote in choosing senators, who instead emerge from the multi-stage professional-group process described above ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

## Legislative Process and Political Weight

Bills generally require passage by both chambers, though the House holds primacy on matters like the budget and, since 2024, on selecting the prime minister without Senate involvement ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Constitutional amendments face a higher bar, typically needing support from a portion of senators as well as opposition-party members of the House — a design intended to prevent any single governing coalition from unilaterally rewriting the charter ([Asialink](https://asialink.unimelb.edu.au/diplomacy/insights/will-thailand-become-more-democratic-after-vote/)).

Because no single party has won a majority in recent elections, the House functions as the primary arena for coalition bargaining. Bhumjaithai's coalition, anchored by Pheu Thai and several smaller parties, held roughly 292 to 296 of the House's 499 filled seats following the 2026 election, giving Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul's government a workable but not unchallengeable majority ([The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/thai-parliament-reconfirms-anutin-charnvirakul-as-prime-minister/)).
