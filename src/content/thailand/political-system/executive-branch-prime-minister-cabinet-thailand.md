# Thailand's Executive Branch: The Prime Minister and Cabinet

*A guide to Thailand's executive branch: how the prime minister is chosen, the cabinet's structure, and current PM Anutin Charnvirakul's second-term government.*

Thailand's executive branch runs on a parliamentary model in which the prime minister, not the king, leads day-to-day government, backed by a cabinet known formally as the Council of Ministers. As of 2026, that role belongs to Anutin Charnvirakul, leader of the Bhumjaithai Party, who became the kingdom's 32nd prime minister in September 2025 and secured a full second term in March 2026 — the first Thai prime minister to be reelected in two decades ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-anutin-seeks-new-mandate-parliament-votes-prime-minister-2026-03-18/)). Understanding how this branch is structured, staffed, and constrained is essential to understanding Thai governance more broadly.

## How the Prime Minister Is Chosen

Under the 2017 constitution, the prime minister is not directly elected by voters. Instead, the position is filled through a vote of the House of Representatives, whose choice is then formally endorsed by royal command ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Historically, the appointed Senate also held a vote on the premiership, a legacy of the 2014 military coup that let junta-picked senators help select the head of government; that senatorial power over the premiership lapsed once the fully indirectly elected 200-member Senate took office in 2024 ([IDEA Democracy Tracker](https://www.idea.int/democracytracker/report/thailand/july-2024)).

Anutin's path to office illustrates how fluid this process can be. He was first elected prime minister by the House in September 2025, a week after the Constitutional Court removed Pheu Thai's Paetongtarn Shinawatra from office, drawing support from lawmakers across party lines with a commitment to hold new elections within months ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/9/5/thai-parliament-elects-anutin-charnvirakul-as-prime-minister-2); [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cr4q051zxgyo)). After Bhumjaithai's decisive win in the February 8, 2026 general election, the House of Representatives reconfirmed Anutin as prime minister on March 19, 2026, with 293 votes against 119 for People's Party leader Natthaphong Ruengpanyawut and 86 abstentions ([Royal Thai Government](https://thailand.prd.go.th/en/content/category/detail/id/48/iid/486890); [The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/thai-parliament-reconfirms-anutin-charnvirakul-as-prime-minister/)).

## The Cabinet: Structure and Current Lineup

The Council of Ministers, headquartered at Government House in Bangkok, spans 20 ministries and is formally appointed by the king on the prime minister's recommendation ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). Anutin's second cabinet — the "Second Anutin cabinet," formally Thailand's 66th Council of Ministers — was endorsed by King Vajiralongkorn on March 30–31, 2026, and comprises 35 members, including seven deputy prime ministers ([Royal Thai Government](https://thailand.prd.go.th/en/content/category/detail/id/2078/iid/490466); [Wikipedia — Second Anutin cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Anutin_cabinet)).

Anutin himself holds a dual role as prime minister and interior minister, consolidating control over domestic security administration ([Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thailands-king-endorses-anutins-new-cabinet-royal-gazette-says)). Key portfolios in the cabinet include:

- **Finance** — Ekniti Nitithanprapas, also a deputy prime minister
- **Foreign Affairs** — Sihasak Phuangketkeow, also a deputy prime minister
- **Commerce** — Suphajee Suthumpun, also a deputy prime minister
- **Transport** — Phipat Ratchakitprakarn, also a deputy prime minister
- **Defence** — Lieutenant General Adul Boonthumjaroen
- **Higher Education, Science, Research and Innovation** — Yodchanan Wongsawat, also a deputy prime minister, and Pheu Thai's top prime ministerial candidate in the 2026 election ([Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thailands-king-endorses-anutins-new-cabinet-royal-gazette-says))

The cabinet reflects a coalition arrangement: Bhumjaithai and its loyalists hold roughly 32 cabinet-linked positions including economic and defense portfolios, while coalition partner Pheu Thai — Thaksin Shinawatra's party — was allotted nine ministerial posts, including agriculture, labor, education, and social development ([Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thailands-king-endorses-anutins-new-cabinet-royal-gazette-says)).

## Powers and Limits of the Executive

The prime minister leads the executive branch but operates under significant checks. Under Section 151 of the 2017 constitution, members of the House of Representatives constituting at least one-fifth of the chamber can submit a no-confidence motion against an individual minister or the cabinet as a whole ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). The judiciary — particularly the Constitutional Court — has also emerged as one of the most powerful checks on the executive in modern Thai politics, having removed two consecutive Pheu Thai prime ministers, Srettha Thavisin in August 2024 and Paetongtarn Shinawatra in August 2025, over ethics and conduct violations ([Asialink](https://asialink.unimelb.edu.au/diplomacy/insights/will-thailand-become-more-democratic-after-vote/)).

Coalition management is another defining feature of executive power in Thailand. Because no single party has won an outright majority of the 500-seat House of Representatives in recent elections, prime ministers must build and maintain multi-party coalitions to pass legislation and survive confidence votes ([Wikipedia — 2026 Thai general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Thai_general_election)). Anutin's governing coalition, anchored by Bhumjaithai's 191–193 seats and Pheu Thai's roughly 74 seats, controls around 292 to 296 of the House's 499 occupied seats, giving the government a workable but not overwhelming majority ([The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/thai-parliament-reconfirms-anutin-charnvirakul-as-prime-minister/); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/politics/40062843)).

## Anutin Charnvirakul: A Profile

Anutin, born in 1966 in Bangkok, is a Thai engineer and businessman who has led the Bhumjaithai Party since 2012 ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Anutin-Charnvirakul)). Before becoming prime minister, he served as deputy prime minister and public health minister during the 2019–2023 government, where he championed the legalization of cannabis, and later as interior minister under the Pheu Thai-led coalition until mid-2025 ([TIME](https://time.com/7314811/anutin-charnvirakul-thailand-prime-minister-bhumjaithai-pheu-thai-peoples-party/)). His rise to the premiership in September 2025 came after Bhumjaithai broke from its coalition with Pheu Thai and secured enough parliamentary backing to take the top job, with the People's Party extracting a promise from Anutin to call fresh elections within four months and pursue amendments to the military-drafted constitution ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cr4q051zxgyo)). He fulfilled that pledge by dissolving parliament on December 11, 2025, and calling the general election that returned him to office with a much stronger mandate ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/news/politics/40062301)).

## Why the Executive Branch Matters

Thailand's executive branch sits at the intersection of electoral politics, royal authority, and judicial oversight — a combination that has produced remarkable turnover at the top: Anutin is the third prime minister in as many years, following the ousters of Srettha Thavisin and Paetongtarn Shinawatra ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cr4q051zxgyo)). His second-term mandate, built on a decisive February 2026 election win, is widely seen as offering a rare chance at governing stability, though it still depends on managing a multi-party coalition and navigating a judiciary and Senate that retain substantial independent power over the executive ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-anutin-seeks-new-mandate-parliament-votes-prime-minister-2026-03-18/)).
