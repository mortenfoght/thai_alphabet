# Thailand in the Mid-2020s: Same-Sex Marriage, Political Crisis, and a New Prime Minister

*Thailand's mid-2020s brought landmark same-sex marriage rights, a leaked-call scandal that toppled a prime minister, and Anutin Charnvirakul's rise to power.*

Few two-year stretches in recent Thai history have packed in as much whiplash as 2024 through early 2026. The country made history as Southeast Asia's first nation to legalize same-sex marriage, then watched a diplomatic phone call implode a sitting government within weeks, before ultimately installing a new prime minister who would go on to win a second term. Thailand in the mid-2020s illustrates both the country's capacity for social progress and the durability of its recurring pattern of political instability, with the judiciary once again playing kingmaker. This period captures Thailand at a genuine inflection point, balancing a historic civil-rights milestone against yet another cycle of governmental upheaval.

## A Historic Milestone: Marriage Equality

Thailand's Marriage Equality Act passed the House of Representatives by an overwhelming 400 to 10 vote on 27 March 2024, followed by Senate approval of 130 to 4 on 18 June 2024 ([Wikipedia — Same-sex marriage in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Thailand)). King Vajiralongkorn granted royal assent on 12 August 2024, and the law was published in the Royal Gazette on 24 September 2024, triggering a 120-day countdown to implementation ([Wikipedia — Same-sex marriage in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Thailand)). The law took effect on 23 January 2025, amending more than 60 sections of the Civil and Commercial Code by replacing gendered terms like "husband" and "wife" with the neutral term "spouse," and extending equal rights covering marriage registration, joint asset management, adoption, and inheritance to same-sex couples ([Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-03-18/thailand-law-recognizing-same-sex-marriage-takes-effect/?loclr=bloglaw)). The legislation also raised the legal marriage age from 17 to 18, aligning Thailand with the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child ([Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-03-18/thailand-law-recognizing-same-sex-marriage-takes-effect/?loclr=bloglaw)).

The change made Thailand the first country in Southeast Asia and only the third in Asia, after Taiwan and Nepal, to legalize same-sex marriage, and the 38th nation worldwide ([Wikipedia — Same-sex marriage in Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Thailand)). Bangkok marked the occasion with a mass wedding event at Siam Paragon organized alongside Bangkok Pride, with the Department of Provincial Administration recording 1,832 same-sex marriages registered nationwide on the law's first day, including 661 in Bangkok alone ([Wikipedia — Marriage Equality Act (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage_Equality_Act_(Thailand))). The UN Human Rights Office welcomed the law as a step toward greater equality and inclusivity for all Thai citizens regardless of gender identity or sexual orientation ([UN Thailand](https://thailand.un.org/en/288067-un-human-rights-office-welcomes-enactment-historic-marriage-equality-law-thailand-legalising)).

## The Cambodia Border Dispute and the Phone Call That Broke a Government

While Thailand celebrated its marriage equality milestone, a long-simmering border dispute with Cambodia was escalating into the defining political crisis of 2025. As tensions rose along the shared frontier, Prime Minister Paetongtarn Shinawatra held a private 17-minute phone call on 15 June 2025 with Cambodian Senate President Hun Sen — a former Cambodian prime minister and longtime associate of her father, Thaksin Shinawatra — intending to discuss a peaceful resolution to the border crisis ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)).

On 18 June 2025, Hun Sen himself leaked a nine-minute recording of the call and confirmed on Facebook that he had recorded the entire conversation ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)). In the recording, Paetongtarn referred to Hun Sen as "uncle," asked him to "please have some sympathy for [his] niece," and criticized a senior Thai military commander, Lieutenant General Boonsin Padklang, for making statements she considered unhelpful and self-serving, describing him as part of the "opposing side" ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)). The leak triggered immediate and widespread condemnation inside Thailand for appearing to disparage the Thai military while flattering a foreign leader amid an active border standoff ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)).

### Coalition Collapse and Market Fallout

The political damage was immediate. The Bhumjaithai Party, a key coalition partner, withdrew from the governing coalition in the wake of the leak, precipitating what became known as the 2025 Thai political crisis ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak); [Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul)). On 23 June 2025, the Stock Exchange of Thailand index fell to its lowest level in more than five years, dipping below 1,063 points as investors reacted to the deepening instability ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)). On 1 July 2025, Thailand's Constitutional Court suspended Paetongtarn from office pending a formal case for her dismissal ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/7/1/court-suspends-thailands-pm-pending-case-over-leaked-phone-call); [Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)). The crisis also claimed a piece of unrelated legislation: on 7 July 2025, a proposed bill to legalize entertainment complexes with casinos was scrapped amid the political turmoil ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak)).

On 29 August 2025, the Constitutional Court ruled 6–3 to formally remove Paetongtarn Shinawatra from the premiership, ending the tenure of Thailand's youngest-ever prime minister barely a year after she had taken office ([Wikipedia — Cambodia–Thailand phone call leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambodia%E2%80%93Thailand_phone_call_leak); [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ckgeqlw05rzo); [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/thailand-politics-prime-minister-dismissed-7720be8eff0b9327fda88aa1285d86f7)). The ruling reaffirmed a now-familiar pattern in Thai politics, in which the Constitutional Court, rather than the military, has become the primary mechanism for removing elected leaders.

## Anutin Charnvirakul's Rise to the Premiership

Anutin Charnvirakul, leader of the Bhumjaithai Party, had built a long political career dating back to 1996, when he entered politics with the Thai Rak Thai party, later serving as Deputy Prime Minister from 2019 and as Minister of Public Health and then Minister of Interior ([Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul)). After Bhumjaithai's June 2025 withdrawal from the coalition helped precipitate Paetongtarn's downfall, Anutin negotiated an agreement with the reformist People's Party — successor to the dissolved Move Forward Party — to secure confidence-and-supply support in exchange for committing to draft a new constitution ([Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul)). The National Assembly elected Anutin as Thailand's 32nd prime minister on 5 September 2025, and King Vajiralongkorn formally endorsed his appointment two days later, on 7 September 2025 ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/9/5/thai-parliament-elects-anutin-charnvirakul-as-prime-minister-2); [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-anutin-charnvirakul-elected-pm-after-rout-ruling-party-rival-2025-09-05/); [Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul)).

### The Snap Election and Second Term

True to his agreement with the People's Party, Anutin dissolved the House of Representatives on 12 December 2025 ahead of a looming no-confidence vote, triggering a snap general election held on 8 February 2026 ([Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul); [Wikipedia — 2026 Thai general election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Thai_general_election)). The Bhumjaithai Party emerged from that election as the largest party in the House of Representatives, winning 194 seats with double-digit gains in both constituency and party-list vote shares ([Wikipedia — Anutin Charnvirakul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anutin_Charnvirakul)). Parliament re-elected Anutin as prime minister in March 2026, and he began his second term with a decisive mandate, having crushed his nearest rival in the parliamentary vote ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/19/thailands-anutin-reelected-pm-after-crushing-rival-in-parliamentary-vote.html); [Thai Government Public Relations Department](https://thailand.prd.go.th/en/content/category/detail/id/48/iid/486890)).

## A Snapshot of Thailand's Political Character

The mid-2020s captured, in condensed form, much of what defines contemporary Thai politics: genuine progress on social issues like marriage equality moving in parallel with familiar instability at the top of government. A leaked private phone call, rather than a battlefield defeat or a public scandal of corruption, proved enough to collapse a sitting government, while the Constitutional Court — not the army — delivered the final verdict. Anutin's subsequent snap election and decisive re-election suggest Thai voters were, at least for the moment, prepared to reward stability over further upheaval, even as the underlying tensions between elected government, the judiciary, and the palace remain unresolved.
