# The 1932 Revolution: How Thailand Became a Constitutional Monarchy

*The 1932 Siamese Revolution was the bloodless coup that ended absolute monarchy and established Thailand's first constitution and parliament.*

On the morning of 24 June 1932, a small group of military officers and civil servants seized control of Bangkok and brought nearly 150 years of absolute Chakri rule to an end within days. The 1932 Revolution, carried out by a group calling itself the Khana Ratsadon, or People's Party, transformed Siam from an absolute monarchy into a constitutional one almost without bloodshed, introducing the country's first constitution and its first national legislature ([Wikipedia — Siamese revolution of 1932](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siamese_revolution_of_1932)). The political framework born that day, though repeatedly amended and interrupted by coups in the decades since, remains the basic model of Thai governance today.

## Siam on the Eve of Revolution

By 1932, King Prajadhipok, known as Rama VII, had ruled since 1925 as the seventh Chakri monarch and the last to hold absolute power ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)). Prajadhipok inherited a kingdom under mounting strain. The Great Depression had battered Siam's economy, forcing the Supreme Council of State to cut civil service payrolls and military budgets, decisions that generated deep resentment inside the bureaucracy and armed forces ([Words for Sam](https://wordsforsam1.wordpress.com/2016/10/12/the-little-house-on-the-beach/); [Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)). At the same time, a new generation of Western-educated Siamese officials and officers, many trained in Europe, had grown frustrated with a system that reserved top government positions for royalty and the old aristocracy ([UCA — Thailand 1932-present](https://uca.edu/politicalscience/home/research-projects/dadm-project/asiapacific-region/thailand-1932-present/); [Britannica — Thailand: 1932 coup](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand/The-1932-coup-and-the-creation-of-a-constitutional-order)).

### The Formation of the Khana Ratsadon

The roots of the revolution stretch back to 1927, when a group of Siamese students and military cadets studying in Paris, including the law student Pridi Phanomyong and the military officer Plaek Phibunsongkhram, founded the Khana Ratsadon, or People's Party — Siam's first political party — dedicated to ending absolute monarchy ([Britannica — Thailand: 1932 coup](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand/The-1932-coup-and-the-creation-of-a-constitutional-order); [German Wikipedia — Pridi Banomyong](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pridi_Banomyong)). On returning to Siam, the group, later known as the Promoters, quietly built support among students, nonroyal government officials, and sympathetic military officers over the following years ([Britannica — Thailand: 1932 coup](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand/The-1932-coup-and-the-creation-of-a-constitutional-order)).

## The Events of 24 June 1932

The coup unfolded while King Prajadhipok was away from Bangkok at his seaside palace, Klai Kangwon, in Hua Hin ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)). Conspirators mobilized military units in the capital, seized control of the Ananta Samakhom Throne Hall, and arrested key royal officials, most of them princes and relatives of the king ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/blogs/news/politics/40051702)). The senior Khana Ratsadon figure Phraya Phahonphonphayuhasena climbed onto a tank in the Royal Plaza and read a manifesto declaring the end of absolute monarchy and the founding of a new constitutional state ([Wikipedia — Phraya Phahonphonphayuhasena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraya_Phahonphonphayuhasena); [Wikipedia — Siamese revolution of 1932](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siamese_revolution_of_1932)).

The Khana Ratsadon's demand was not the abolition of the monarchy itself but its conversion into a constitutional institution: the king would remain head of state, but under a written constitution and an elected government, and if Prajadhipok refused, the plotters reserved the right to declare Siam a republic ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)). The coup succeeded largely because it drew on the support of army units commanded by nonroyal officers, allowing it to proceed with almost no violence ([Britannica — Thailand: 1932 coup](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand/The-1932-coup-and-the-creation-of-a-constitutional-order)).

### The King's Response

King Prajadhipok returned to Bangkok by royal train on the night of 25 June 1932. The following day, he met the People's Party leaders and, after initial hesitation, agreed to their terms ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/blogs/news/politics/40051702); [Wikipedia — Siamese revolution of 1932](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siamese_revolution_of_1932)). He signed a decree granting amnesty to everyone involved in the coup and, on 27 June 1932, promulgated a provisional constitution drafted in advance by Pridi Phanomyong ([Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/blogs/news/politics/40051702); [Constitution of Thailand — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Thailand)).

## Building Siam's First Constitutional Order

The provisional charter signed on 27 June 1932 opened with the declaration that "the highest power in the land belongs to all the people," stripping the king of his traditional powers of veto, pardon, and even the right to name his own successor ([Constitution of Thailand — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Thailand)). It created a People's Assembly as the legislature, a People's Committee as the executive, and preserved the courts as an independent judiciary, alongside the monarchy itself, which continued to exist as an institution but with its powers hollowed out ([Constitution of Thailand — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Thailand); World History — Siamese revolution of 1932 blog).

### The Permanent Constitution of December 1932

The provisional charter was always intended as a stopgap. On 10 December 1932, King Prajadhipok signed Siam's first permanent constitution, a date still commemorated in Thailand as Constitution Day ([Thai Senate](https://www.senate.go.th/assets/portals/1/fileups/421/files/%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%A7%E0%B8%A1%E0%B9%84%E0%B8%9F%E0%B8%A5%E0%B9%8C%E0%B8%AB%E0%B8%99%E0%B8%B1%E0%B8%87%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%B7%E0%B8%AD%E0%B8%A7%E0%B8%B8%E0%B8%92%E0%B8%B4%E0%B8%AA%E0%B8%A0%E0%B8%B2%204%20%E0%B8%A0%E0%B8%B2%E0%B8%A9%E0%B8%B2.pdf)). This permanent charter restored some prestige to the crown compared with the provisional charter, again describing the monarchy as "sacred and inviolable," and expanded the legislature to 156 members split between elected and appointed seats, with Siam's first general election scheduled for October 1933 ([World History — Siamese revolution of 1932 blog](https://thinkforreform.wordpress.com/2015/09/05/siamese-revolution-of-1932/)).

### Political Struggles After the Revolution

The new order proved unstable almost immediately. Divisions emerged within the Khana Ratsadon itself among a senior military faction led by Phraya Phahon, a junior military faction led by Phibunsongkhram, and a civilian faction led by Pridi ([Wikipedia — Phraya Phahonphonphayuhasena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraya_Phahonphonphayuhasena)). Within a year, a further coup in April 1933 unseated the government of Prime Minister Phraya Manopakorn, underscoring how quickly the promise of stable constitutional rule gave way to a recurring pattern of military intervention ([Wikipedia — April 1933 Siamese coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/April_1933_Siamese_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat)). Relations between the crown and the People's Party continued to deteriorate, and on 2 March 1935, Prajadhipok abdicated from exile in England, having concluded that the new government would not restore the powers or reforms he had hoped to see ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)). He was succeeded by his young nephew, Ananda Mahidol, as Rama VIII ([Wikipedia — Prajadhipok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajadhipok)).

## A Lasting Political Legacy

The 1932 Revolution did not deliver the stable liberal democracy its architects envisioned, but it permanently altered the structure of Thai governance. Absolute monarchy was gone for good, replaced by a framework — however imperfect and frequently suspended by later coups — built around a constitution, a national assembly, and a monarch serving as head of state rather than sole ruler. Even as Thailand has cycled through roughly twenty constitutions since 1932, the basic principle established that June morning, that governing authority flows from a constitutional order rather than from the person of the king alone, has endured as the foundation of the modern Thai state ([Constitution of Thailand — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Thailand)).
