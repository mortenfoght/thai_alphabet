# Coups and Constitutions: Thailand's Cycle of Political Instability

*Trace the pattern behind Thailand's coups and constitutions, from the 1932 revolution to the 2014 takeover, and why the cycle keeps repeating itself.*

Few modern nations have rewritten their fundamental law as often, or been upended by their own armed forces as regularly, as Thailand. Since the end of absolute monarchy in 1932, the country has cycled through roughly 20 constitutions and at least a dozen successful military coups, alongside numerous failed attempts — a record that makes Thailand something of an outlier even among politically volatile states ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/country/thailand)). Understanding this recurring pattern of coups and constitutions is essential to understanding Thai politics today, where the military, the judiciary, and elected governments remain locked in an uneasy, decades-long balance of power.

## The Origins of the Cycle: 1932

The pattern began with the Siamese revolution of 24 June 1932, when the Khana Ratsadon, or People's Party, staged a bloodless coup that ended nearly 150 years of absolute monarchy and established Thailand's first constitution ([Wikipedia — History of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Thailand)). What followed was not a smooth transition to stable parliamentary democracy but the beginning of a recurring struggle between military factions, civilian politicians, and the palace over who would truly hold power. Between 1932 and 1991 alone, Thailand experienced ten military coups, eight of which directly resulted in new constitutions ([ConstitutionNet](https://constitutionnet.org/country/thailand)).

## Early Postwar Instability

The name change from Siam to Thailand in 1939, the wartime alliance with Japan, and the brief postwar reversion to the name Siam in 1945 all took place against a backdrop of shifting military and civilian control ([Wikipedia — History of Thailand (1932–1973)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Thailand_(1932%E2%80%931973))). Plaek Phibunsongkhram returned to power through a 1947 coup, restoring the Thailand name permanently in 1949 and beginning a long era in which military strongmen — first Phibun, then Sarit Thanarat, then Thanom Kittikachorn — dominated Thai governance while rewriting the constitution to suit each new regime ([Wikipedia — History of Thailand (1932–1973)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Thailand_(1932%E2%80%931973))). During King Bhumibol Adulyadej's 70-year reign from 1946 to 2016, Thailand experienced ten separate coups and seventeen constitutions, illustrating just how routine the cycle had become even under a single, long-reigning monarch ([Reuters](https://jp.reuters.com/article/world/timeline-thailands-coups-kings-and-protests-idUSKBN28S1BZ/)).

## The 1991 Coup and Black May 1992

A coup in February 1991 installed the National Peace Keeping Council, which governed until March 1992, when coup leader General Suchinda Kraprayoon controversially assumed the prime ministership himself despite earlier promises not to do so ([tci-thaijo](https://so04.tci-thaijo.org/index.php/NAJUA/article/download/274463/186499/1159417)). Suchinda's move triggered mass protests at Sanam Luang led by Major General Chamlong Srimuang, culminating in the violent crackdown known as Black May, in which official counts recorded 52 deaths, hundreds of injuries, and 175 disappearances between 17 and 20 May 1992, though unofficial estimates ran considerably higher ([Wikipedia — Black May (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_May_(Thailand))). King Bhumibol personally intervened, summoning Suchinda and Chamlong for a televised audience that led to Suchinda's resignation and eventually paved the way for the reformist 1997 constitution, often called the "People's Constitution" for its comparatively democratic, civil-society-driven drafting process ([tci-thaijo](https://so04.tci-thaijo.org/index.php/NAJUA/article/download/274463/186499/1159417)).

## The Thaksin Era and the 2006 Coup

The 1997 constitution ushered in a period of unusually stable, majoritarian government under Thaksin Shinawatra's Thai Rak Thai party, but this stability provoked its own backlash. Mass demonstrations by the People's Alliance for Democracy against Thaksin's government grew through 2006, and on the evening of 19 September 2006, while Thaksin was in New York to address a UN summit, the Royal Thai Army staged a coup that dissolved the 1997 constitution, suspended the Senate, and installed a junta calling itself the Council for National Security ([Wikipedia — 2006 Thai coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Thai_coup_d'%C3%A9tat); [ABC News Australia](https://www.abc.net.au/news/2006-09-20/military-leaders-take-control-in-bloodless-thai/1267746)). It was Thailand's first coup in fifteen years and, by the count of many scholars, the eighteenth coup since 1932 ([Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/good-coup-gone-bad/good-coup-gone-bad-thailands-political-developments-since-thaksins-downfall/B3EB53985B089D91C11F60164F6A75C2)). A junta-drafted constitution was approved by referendum in August 2007, becoming Thailand's 18th such document in 75 years of intermittent democracy ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/article/world/-thailand-since-2006-coup-ousted-thaksin-idUSBKK59066/)).

## The 2014 Coup and the 2017 Constitution

Political conflict between Thaksin-aligned and anti-Thaksin factions resurfaced repeatedly over the following years, culminating in another military takeover. On 22 May 2014, General Prayut Chan-o-cha, then commander-in-chief of the Royal Thai Army, led a coup against the caretaker government and established the National Council for Peace and Order (NCPO), which governed the country directly until July 2019 ([Wikipedia — Prayut Chan-o-cha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prayut_Chan-o-cha); [Human Rights Watch](https://www.hrw.org/news/2017/05/22/thailand-junta-entrenched-3-years-after-coup)). The NCPO first governed under a 2014 interim constitution before a new, permanent charter — drafted with close involvement from King Vajiralongkorn following his 2016 accession — was promulgated on 6 April 2017, becoming Thailand's 20th constitution since 1932 ([The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2017/09/new-king-deep-state-thailand/); [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2017/apr/06/thailand-king-signs-constitution-path-polls-election)). Notably, the king revised the draft charter after its approval by public referendum in August 2016, specifically to strengthen royal authority within the new constitutional order before it took effect ([The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2017/09/new-king-deep-state-thailand/)).

## Constitutional Courts as a Modern Substitute for Tanks

Since the mid-2000s, Thailand's Constitutional Court has increasingly performed a role once reserved for military coups: removing sitting prime ministers and dissolving political parties through judicial rulings rather than armed intervention ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)). The court has dissolved political parties on nine separate occasions since 2007, most notably ordering the dissolution of the Move Forward Party in August 2024 over its campaign to reform Thailand's lèse-majesté law, and banning ten of its executives, including former leader Pita Limjaroenrat, from politics for a decade ([House of Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10141/)). This judicial mechanism has effectively become a parallel channel for the same kind of establishment-driven political resets that coups once delivered directly.

## Why the Cycle Persists

Several structural features help explain why Thailand keeps repeating this pattern rather than settling into durable constitutional government. The military retains deep institutional autonomy and a self-appointed role as guardian of the monarchy and national unity, a justification invoked in nearly every coup since 1932 ([European Parliament](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2020/659330/EPRS_ATA(2020)659330_EN.pdf)). Each new constitution, rather than resolving the underlying tension between elected governments and unelected institutions, tends instead to encode the interests of whichever faction happens to be drafting it — explaining why democratic-leaning charters like the 1997 constitution and military-favoring charters like the 2017 constitution have alternated over time. The result is what the Economist Intelligence Unit has termed a "flawed democracy" and what the V-Dem Democracy Indices describe as an "electoral autocracy," reflecting how deeply military and judicial institutions remain embedded in Thai governance even when civilians nominally hold office ([Wikipedia — Politics of Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_Thailand)).

## A Pattern With No Clear End

As of the mid-2020s, Thailand continues to operate under the 2017 constitution, even as political turbulence — including the 2025 removal of Prime Minister Paetongtarn Shinawatra by the Constitutional Court — demonstrates that the underlying cycle of instability has not disappeared, even without another outright military takeover ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ckgeqlw05rzo)). Commentators covering Thai politics have long noted that in a country that has seen at least 20 military coups, no one would confidently bet against another one occurring eventually ([BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-63082400)).
