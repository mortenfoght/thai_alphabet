# The Chakri Dynasty and the Founding of Bangkok

*The Chakri Dynasty has ruled Thailand since 1782, when Rama I founded Bangkok as Siam's capital after the fall of Ayutthaya.*

The Chakri dynasty, which still occupies the Thai throne today, traces its origins to the chaos that followed the destruction of Ayutthaya in 1767. Out of that crisis emerged first a soldier-king who briefly reunified Siam, and then a new royal house that built Bangkok from a riverside village into the seat of a dynasty now more than two centuries old ([Wikipedia — Chakri dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakri_dynasty)). Understanding how the Chakri dynasty came to power, and why its founder chose the site of modern Bangkok, requires starting with the general who first pulled Siam back from collapse: Taksin.

## Taksin and the Kingdom of Thonburi

When Ayutthaya fell to Burmese forces in April 1767, Siam splintered into rival regional power centers with no central authority ([Wikipedia — Siege of Ayutthaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Ayutthaya)). A military officer named Phraya Tak, later known as Taksin, moved quickly to fill the vacuum. He led naval forces along the Gulf of Thailand, recaptured the area around the old capital from the Burmese, and by 1770 had subjugated rival claimants to reunify most of Siam under his authority ([Taksin's reunification of Siam — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taksin%27s_reunification_of_Siam)).

Rather than rebuild at devastated Ayutthaya, Taksin established a new capital at Thonburi, on the west bank of the Chao Phraya River, choosing a site closer to the Gulf of Siam that would better serve seaborne commerce ([Britannica — Thon Buri and Early Bangkok periods](https://www.britannica.com/place/Thailand/The-Thon-Buri-and-Early-Bangkok-periods); [Taksin's reunification of Siam — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taksin%27s_reunification_of_Siam)). He was crowned king in 1768 and, over the following years, extended Siamese power into Chiang Mai, Laos, and Cambodia ([Britannica — Taksin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Taksin)). The Thonburi Kingdom lasted only fourteen years. According to the Royal Chronicles, Taksin's later reign was marked by instability, and in 1782 a rebellion broke out in the capital amid internal conflicts over treasure and succession ([Wikipedia — Thonburi Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thonburi_Kingdom)).

## Rama I and the Founding of a New Dynasty

At the time of the 1782 rebellion, Taksin's leading general, Chao Phraya Chakri, was away campaigning in Cambodia. He returned to Thonburi, took control of the unrest, and was offered the throne by the rebellion's leaders ([Britannica — Rama I](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rama-I); [Andy Barca](https://www.andybarca.com/blog/rama-i-coronation-1782/)). Taksin was deposed and executed, and Chao Phraya Chakri ascended the throne on 6 April 1782, founding the Chakri dynasty under the regnal name Ramathibodi — known to history as Rama I ([Wikipedia — Chakri dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakri_dynasty); [Wikipedia — Rama I, Simple English](https://simple.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rama_I)).

### Why Bangkok?

One of Rama I's first acts as king was to relocate the capital across the Chao Phraya River, from Thonburi on the west bank to a trading settlement called Bang Makok on the east bank ([Wikipedia — Rattanakosin Kingdom (1782–1932)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom_(1782%E2%80%931932))). The move served multiple purposes: the east bank offered a stronger defensive position, and starting fresh on new ground helped the new king assert his own legitimacy rather than governing from a site tied to his predecessor's downfall ([NamuWiki](https://en.namu.wiki/w/%EB%9D%BC%EB%A7%88%201%EC%84%B8); [Andy Barca](https://www.andybarca.com/blog/rama-i-coronation-1782/)).

The city pillar marking the new capital's foundation was consecrated on 21 April 1782, the date now recognized as the official founding of Bangkok ([Wikipedia — Rattanakosin Kingdom (1782–1932)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom_(1782%E2%80%931932))). Rama I underwent an abbreviated coronation that year and a full coronation ceremony in 1785, at which point he formally named the new city "Rattanakosin," meaning the "Jewel of Indra," a reference to the Emerald Buddha housed there ([Wikipedia — Rattanakosin Kingdom (1782–1932)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom_(1782%E2%80%931932))). Foreigners continued to call the city Bangkok, the name of the original riverside village, and that name eventually became standard internationally ([Made in Thailand](https://madeinthai.wordpress.com/2011/04/20/the-nine-kings-of-the-chakri-dynasty-rama-i-%E2%80%93-the-founding-father-2/)).

### Building the New Capital

Rama I modeled the layout and institutions of his new capital closely on the lost city of Ayutthaya, aiming to restore continuity with Siam's pre-1767 golden age ([Made in Thailand](https://madeinthai.wordpress.com/2011/04/20/the-nine-kings-of-the-chakri-dynasty-rama-i-%E2%80%93-the-founding-father-2/)). Construction of the Grand Palace and temple complex proceeded rapidly, and Rama I undertook a broad renovation of the kingdom's legal and religious institutions, compiling revised law codes and reasserting the prestige of the monarchy after years of upheaval ([Britannica — Rama I](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rama-I)). During his 27-year reign, he also repelled the last major Burmese invasion of Siamese territory, fought during the Nine Armies' War of 1785–1786 ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/article/world/factbox-the-10-ramas-the-kings-of-thailands-chakri-dynasty-idUSKCN1S80UR/)).

## The Early Chakri Kings

Rama I died in 1809 after 27 years on the throne, having transformed a river-bank trading post into a functioning capital and secured his dynasty's hold on power ([Andy Barca](https://www.andybarca.com/blog/rama-i-coronation-1782/)). He was succeeded by his son, who took the name Rama II and reigned until 1824, continuing the work of stabilizing the kingdom and expanding its cultural life ([Wikipedia — Rama II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rama_II)). Rama III followed, ruling from 1824 to 1851, a period during which Siam continued to consolidate its territory and manage growing contact with European trading powers ([Wikipedia — Rama III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rama_III)).

This early Rattanakosin period — the historical term for the era spanning the first seven Chakri kings, from the founding of Bangkok in 1782 to the end of the absolute monarchy in 1932 — established the political and religious framework that would carry Siam into the more turbulent 19th century, when European colonial pressure across Southeast Asia intensified dramatically ([Wikipedia — Rattanakosin Kingdom (1782–1932)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom_(1782%E2%80%931932))).

## A Dynasty That Endures

The Chakri dynasty Rama I founded in 1782 has now reigned for more than two centuries, surviving the transition from absolute to constitutional monarchy in 1932, foreign wars, and repeated political upheaval ([Wikipedia — Chakri dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakri_dynasty)). Bangkok, chosen almost by necessity on the east bank of the Chao Phraya in 1782, remains Thailand's capital and the seat of the Chakri kings to this day ([Andy Barca](https://www.andybarca.com/blog/rama-i-coronation-1782/)).
