# Modern Political History of Thailand: From the 1970s Uprisings to King Vajiralongkorn's Succession

*A complete guide to modern Thailand's political history, covering the 1973 and 1976 uprisings, Black May, the Thaksin era, the 2014 coup, and King Vajiralongkorn's succession.*

Modern Thailand's political history reads like a long argument between competing visions of the nation — one favoring elected, civilian government and the other favoring military and royalist oversight — an argument punctuated by street uprisings, massacres, coups, and, more recently, court rulings. From the student-led revolt that toppled a military dictatorship in 1973 to the death of King Bhumibol Adulyadej in 2016 and the eventual coronation of his son as King Vajiralongkorn, the roughly five decades between these events shaped nearly every institution governing Thailand today. This modern political history explains how the country moved from military dictatorship toward fragile democracy, and why that democracy has never fully escaped the shadow of the armed forces and the palace.

## The 1973 Popular Uprising

By the early 1970s, Thailand had been governed for over a decade by the military dictatorship of Field Marshal Thanom Kittikachorn, whose regime grew increasingly unpopular amid economic strain and a corruption scandal involving illegal hunting by military officials known as the Thung Yai affair ([Wikipedia — 1973 Thai popular uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_Thai_popular_uprising)). Between 9 and 15 October 1973, university students and their sympathizers, joined eventually by more than 500,000 demonstrators, staged sit-ins and protest marches along Ratchadamnoen Avenue in Bangkok demanding Thanom's resignation and a new permanent constitution ([Wikipedia — 1973 Thai popular uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_Thai_popular_uprising)). Seventy-seven people died and 857 were injured in the unrest before King Bhumibol announced on television and radio that Thanom's government had resigned, ending the military dictatorship and installing the civilian jurist Sanya Dharmasakti as prime minister ([Wikipedia — 1973 Thai popular uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_Thai_popular_uprising); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/thailand/politics/40020756)).

## Chaotic Democracy and the 6 October 1976 Massacre

The three years following the 1973 uprising, sometimes called Thailand's period of "chaotic democracy," saw an explosion of political organizing among students, workers, and farmers, alongside growing alarm on the political right — fueled by the communist victories in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia in 1975 ([Wikipedia — 6 October 1976 massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6_October_1976_massacre)). Tensions peaked when Thanom returned from exile in August 1976, prompting mass protests at Thammasat University. On 6 October 1976, Thai police and right-wing paramilitary groups, including the Village Scouts, Nawaphon, and Red Gaurs, stormed the campus using war-grade weapons, killing protesting students and lynching others as they attempted to flee ([Wikipedia — 6 October 1976 massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6_October_1976_massacre)). Official government figures put the death toll at 46, with 167 wounded and over 3,000 arrested, though unofficial estimates from groups that transported and cremated bodies suggest the true toll exceeded 100 ([Wikipedia — 6 October 1976 massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6_October_1976_massacre); [Nation Thailand](https://www.nationthailand.com/thailand/politics/40020756)). The massacre was followed that same evening by a military coup, abruptly ending the three-year democratic experiment ([Wikipedia — 6 October 1976 massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6_October_1976_massacre)).

## Black May 1992

Thailand's next major democratic rupture came after a February 1991 coup installed the National Peace Keeping Council, which governed until General Suchinda Kraprayoon assumed the prime ministership in April 1992 despite prior assurances that he would not seek the post ([Wikipedia — Black May (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_May_(Thailand))). Mass protests led by Major General Chamlong Srimuang, the largest since 1976, gathered at Sanam Luang starting 17 May 1992. Security forces cracked down violently over the following days, with official statements citing 52 deaths, nearly 700 injuries, and 175 disappearances, while medical volunteers and the New York Times reported the true toll may have exceeded 100 ([Wikipedia — Black May (Thailand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_May_(Thailand))). King Bhumibol summoned Suchinda and Chamlong for a televised royal audience that forced Suchinda's resignation, a moment widely credited with paving the way for the democratic reforms embodied in Thailand's 1997 "People's Constitution."

## The Rise and Fall of Thaksin Shinawatra

The 1997 constitution helped produce Thailand's first genuinely dominant elected government under telecommunications billionaire Thaksin Shinawatra and his Thai Rak Thai party, whose populist policies won broad support in rural Thailand. Opposition to Thaksin mounted through 2005 and 2006 via the People's Alliance for Democracy, and on 19 September 2006, while Thaksin addressed the United Nations in New York, the Royal Thai Army staged a coup that dissolved his government, suspended the constitution, and installed a military-led council ([Wikipedia — 2006 Thai coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_Thai_coup_d'%C3%A9tat)). The coup did not end Thaksin's political influence; parties aligned with him, later rebranded Pheu Thai, continued winning elections over the following years, sustaining a bitter, colour-coded rivalry between pro-Thaksin "Red Shirts" and anti-Thaksin "Yellow Shirts" that defined Thai street politics for much of the following decade.

## The 2014 Coup and Prayut Chan-o-cha

Renewed political deadlock led to a second major military intervention on 22 May 2014, when General Prayut Chan-o-cha led a coup establishing the National Council for Peace and Order, which ruled Thailand directly until July 2019 ([Wikipedia — Prayut Chan-o-cha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prayut_Chan-o-cha)). The junta governed initially under a 2014 interim charter before promulgating a new, permanent constitution on 6 April 2017 — Thailand's 20th since 1932 — following a 2016 public referendum and subsequent revisions requested by the newly enthroned King Vajiralongkorn ([The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2017/apr/06/thailand-king-signs-constitution-path-polls-election); [The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2017/09/new-king-deep-state-thailand/)).

## The Death of King Bhumibol and Vajiralongkorn's Succession

King Bhumibol Adulyadej, who had reigned since 1946 and witnessed roughly twenty coups, attempted and successful, died on 13 October 2016 after 70 years on the throne, plunging the nation into deep mourning ([The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/oct/13/thai-king-bhumibol-adulyadej-dies-after-70-year-reign)). His only son, Crown Prince Vajiralongkorn, initially delayed formally ascending the throne, accepting the accession invitation from Privy Council president Prem Tinsulanonda only on 1 December 2016, though the government retroactively dated the start of his reign to his father's death ([Wikipedia — Vajiralongkorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vajiralongkorn); [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/oct/16/thailand-crown-prince-king-bhumibol-adulyadej)). Vajiralongkorn proved considerably more assertive than his father regarding direct control of royal institutions: in 2017 he requested revisions to the draft constitution to strengthen monarchical authority, and in 2018 he was granted personal ownership of roughly $40 billion in assets previously held by the Crown Property Bureau on behalf of the public ([Wikipedia — 2020–2021 Thai protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%932021_Thai_protests)).

His formal coronation, the first in Thailand in nearly seven decades, took place over three days from 4 to 6 May 2019 at Bangkok's Grand Palace, blending Buddhist and Brahmin Hindu rites, and following his marriage to Queen Suthida just three days prior ([Wikipedia — Coronation of Vajiralongkorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation_of_Vajiralongkorn); [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/may/04/thailands-king-vajiralongkorn-is-crowned-as-three-day-coronation-begins)). The ceremony came just over a month after Thailand's first general election in eight years, intended to mark a symbolic return to democracy following five years of junta rule ([The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/may/04/thailands-king-vajiralongkorn-is-crowned-as-three-day-coronation-begins)).

## Youth Protests and Calls for Monarchy Reform

Vajiralongkorn's consolidation of royal power, combined with lingering anger over the 2014 coup and Prayut's continued grip on government, fueled the unprecedented 2020–2021 Thai protests, in which student-led demonstrators openly called for reform of the monarchy — a previously taboo subject given Thailand's strict lèse-majesté laws ([Wikipedia — 2020–2021 Thai protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%932021_Thai_protests)). Protesters specifically demanded reversal of the 2017 constitutional changes that had expanded royal authority since Vajiralongkorn's accession ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/americas/why-thai-protesters-are-challenging-monarchy-2020-09-20/)). Though the protest movement did not achieve its core demands, it permanently altered public discourse around the monarchy and set the stage for the reformist Move Forward Party's breakthrough performance in the 2023 general election.

## From Election Victory to Court Dissolution

Move Forward won the most seats of any party in the May 2023 election but was blocked from forming a government after the conservative, military-appointed Senate refused to endorse its candidate, Pita Limjaroenrat, for prime minister. Pheu Thai instead formed a coalition with former military-aligned rivals, installing Srettha Thavisin as prime minister on 22 August 2023 ([Wikipedia — Srettha Thavisin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Srettha_Thavisin)). The Constitutional Court removed Srettha from office in August 2024 over ethics violations, and days later the court separately ordered the dissolution of Move Forward itself, banning ten executives including Pita from politics for a decade over the party's campaign to reform the lèse-majesté law ([House of Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10141/)). Paetongtarn Shinawatra, Thaksin's daughter, succeeded Srettha as Thailand's youngest-ever prime minister, only to be removed herself by the Constitutional Court in August 2025 following a diplomatic scandal — underscoring how, even decades after the 1970s uprisings, Thailand's courts and unelected institutions continue to shape political outcomes as decisively as the ballot box.
